# Humidity Setpoint Optimization: Keeping South Florida Homes Comfortable in Peak Summer

Most homeowners think about temperature when setting their thermostat. But in South Florida, humidity is arguably the more important variable for comfort, indoor air quality, and system efficiency from June through September. This guide explains how to optimize your AC's humidity control during peak summer.

## Why Humidity Matters More in South Florida

Ambient dew points in Miami-Dade and Broward counties regularly exceed 72°F during summer months. At these levels, the air is physically saturated — sweat cannot evaporate from skin, making 76°F feel oppressive even though the thermostat reads a reasonable number. A well-configured AC system acts as a dehumidifier first and a cooler second.

## The Temperature/Humidity Tradeoff

Standard thermostat setpoints (72–74°F) during peak summer often give acceptable temperature readings but poor humidity control. Here's why:

- **Short cycling at low setpoints**: When an oversized (or properly sized) system reaches 72°F quickly, it shuts off before the evaporator coil has condensed enough moisture from the air.
- **Latent vs. sensible cooling**: The AC's capacity splits between sensible cooling (lowering temperature) and latent cooling (removing humidity). A system that satisfies the thermostat quickly has done mostly sensible work, leaving latent moisture in the air.
- **The 75/50 rule**: Indoor relative humidity above 50% at 75°F feels clammy and promotes mold spore growth. Below 45% can dry out nasal passages. The sweet zone is 45–50% RH.

## Optimal Setpoint Strategy for Summer

### Recommended Settings

| Condition | Thermostat Setpoint | Expected RH | Comfort |
|-----------|-------------------|-------------|---------|
| Peak heat (95°F+) | 76–78°F | 50–55% | Good with ceiling fans |
| Moderate (85–95°F) | 75–77°F | 45–50% | Excellent |
| Overcast/rainy | 74–76°F | 48–52% | Good — longer run times help dehumidify |
| Nighttime | 74–75°F | 50–55% | Comfortable for sleep |

**Critical insight**: Set the thermostat 2–3°F *warmer* than you think you need, with "auto" fan mode (not "on"). This forces longer compressor run cycles that wick more moisture from the air. The higher temperature reads the same on the thermometer but *feels* more comfortable because of lower humidity.

## Fan Mode: Auto vs. On

This single setting has an outsized impact on humidity control:

- **Fan ON**: The blower continues running even after the compressor cycles off. This re-evaporates condensation from the evaporator coil back into the airstream, raising indoor RH by 5–10 percentage points.
- **Fan AUTO**: The blower runs only when the compressor runs. Condensation drains away and does not return to the indoor air.

For South Florida summer, **always use Fan AUTO**. Reserve Fan ON for air circulation when the system is not cooling (spring/fall shoulder seasons).

## Tools That Help

### Smart Thermostat with Dehumidify Capability

Thermostats like the Ecobee and higher-end Honeywell models have a "dehumidify using AC" or "overcool" feature. Set a target RH (50%) and a maximum over-cool limit (2–3°F). The thermostat will run the AC beyond the temperature setpoint by up to the over-cool limit if humidity is above target. This is highly effective in South Florida's shoulder months (May, October) when moderate temperatures but high humidity make standard thermostat logic ineffective.

### Whole-House Dehumidifier

For homes that maintain good temperature but still feel humid (common in well-shaded or north-facing houses), a whole-house dehumidifier tied into the HVAC ductwork is the definitive solution. These units operate independently of the AC, pulling moisture without overcooling. Expect $1,500–$2,500 installed — worthwhile for homeowners sensitive to mold or humidity.

### Standalone Monitoring

A simple $15–30 digital hygrometer in the main living area gives real-time RH readings. Many homeowners discover their "75°F feels muggy" problem is actually 75°F at 62% RH — which should feel fine but doesn't because the thermostat reading is at the hallway thermostat, not the living room.

## Signs Your AC Isn't Dehumidifying Properly

- Windows fog on the interior during summer mornings
- Musty smell develops in carpet or upholstery
- Visible mold on air supply vents
- AC runs but feels cool and clammy, not cool and dry
- Thermostat shows 74°F but occupants complain of stickiness

## When Oversizing Is the Real Problem

If your AC system is too large for the home (common in South Florida where builders install one-size-fits-all 4-ton units for 1,800 sq ft homes), it will *never* dehumidify properly because it satisfies the thermostat too quickly. The only remedies are:

- A variable-speed system that reduces capacity for longer run times
- A whole-house dehumidifier
- Accepting a lower thermostat setpoint (which increases electric bills)
