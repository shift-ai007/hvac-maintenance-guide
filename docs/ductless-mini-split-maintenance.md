# Ductless Mini-Split Maintenance for South Florida Homes

Ductless mini-split systems have quietly become one of the most popular cooling solutions in South Florida. They show up in converted garages, home additions, sunrooms, detached in-law suites (ADUs), condos where ductwork can't be run, and any room the central system never quite reaches. They're efficient, quiet, and zone-controllable — but they are **not** maintenance-free, and the humid, salt-laden coastal climate is unusually hard on them.

This guide explains what a mini-split actually needs, what you can do yourself, and where the line is between homeowner upkeep and professional service.

## How a Mini-Split Differs From Central AC

A mini-split has two main parts: an **outdoor condenser** (the same idea as a central system's outdoor unit) and one or more **indoor air handlers** (the wall-, ceiling-, or floor-mounted "heads"). Refrigerant lines and a condensate drain connect them through a small wall penetration.

The key maintenance difference is the indoor head. On a central system, the evaporator coil and blower are hidden in an air handler with a replaceable 1-inch filter. On a mini-split, the coil, blower wheel, and a pair of thin washable mesh filters all live inside that slim wall unit — right in your living space, pulling humid air across cold metal hour after hour. That makes the indoor head a magnet for biofilm, mold, and the dreaded "dirty sock" smell if it isn't cleaned.

## The Monthly Homeowner Routine

These tasks are genuinely DIY and take about 15 minutes per head:

1. **Pop and rinse the mesh filters.** Open the front cover of the indoor head (it hinges up on most models), lift out the two thin plastic-mesh filters, rinse them under lukewarm water, let them dry fully, and reinsert. In South Florida's dust and pollen, monthly is the right cadence — not quarterly.
2. **Wipe the intake louvers and the visible front of the coil.** A damp microfiber cloth removes the film that feeds mold growth.
3. **Glance at the condensate path.** Mini-split condensate usually drains by gravity through a small flexible line that exits the wall. Confirm it's actually dripping outside during operation and that the outdoor end isn't kinked, buried in mulch, or colonized by insects.

## What Builds Up Inside the Head (and Why It Smells)

The blower wheel — a long cylindrical fan inside the head — accumulates a black-green slime of dust, condensate, and microbial growth. Those mesh filters only catch large particles; the fine stuff cakes the wheel and the deep coil fins. Once that biofilm establishes, you get musty odors every time the unit starts, reduced airflow, and lower efficiency.

You **cannot** fully clean this with a rag. Proper service means a "bib wash" — a technician bags the head, sprays a coil cleaner and bio-treatment, and flushes the slurry into a bucket — or in bad cases, removing the head from the wall entirely. This is the single most-skipped mini-split service and the number-one reason owners think the unit "smells like a basement." ## Coastal and Climate-Specific Concerns

- **Salt corrosion on the outdoor unit.** If you're within a few miles of the coast, the mini-split condenser corrodes just like a central unit. Rinse it with fresh water periodically and keep an eye on the aluminum fins and electrical terminals.
- **Condensate volume is enormous here.** Our humidity means a mini-split pulls a remarkable amount of water out of the air. A gravity drain that's slightly clogged will back up into the head and drip down your wall — a very common call. Some installs use a small condensate pump; if yours does, that pump is a wear item.
- **Mounting and storm exposure.** Outdoor mini-split condensers are often wall-mounted on brackets. Before hurricane season, verify the brackets and fasteners are sound and corrosion-free.
- **Refrigerant line insulation.** The foam insulation wrapping the line set degrades fast in UV and heat. Cracked, sun-rotted insulation causes sweating, dripping, and efficiency loss — an easy thing to catch on a visual check.

## Annual Professional Service

Once a year, a licensed technician should:

- Deep-clean the indoor coil and blower wheel (bib wash)
- Clean and rinse the outdoor condenser coil
- Verify refrigerant charge via superheat/subcooling (mini-splits are charge-sensitive)
- Flush and treat the condensate path; test any condensate pump
- Tighten electrical connections and inspect the line-set insulation
- Confirm the inverter/control board has no stored fault codes

Mini-splits use inverter-driven variable-speed compressors, which are efficient but unforgiving of low charge or restricted airflow. Getting the charge and airflow verified by a pro protects the most expensive component in the system.

## When to Call a Professional

Stop and call a technician if you notice:

- A persistent musty or sour smell that returns right after you clean the filters (biofilm on the blower wheel)
- Water dripping from the bottom of the indoor head onto the wall or floor (drain backup)
- Ice forming on the indoor coil or the refrigerant lines (low charge or airflow restriction — never DIY)
- Error codes flashing on the head or remote, or the unit cycling off on its own
- Weak airflow even with clean filters
- Any oily residue near the refrigerant fittings (a leak)
