# Summer Vacation AC Settings for South Florida Homeowners

Planning a summer getaway from Miami, Fort Lauderdale, or Palm Beach? What you do with your air conditioner before you leave can mean the difference between coming home to a comfortable house—and returning to a mold-infested, humidity-damaged nightmare.

South Florida's unique climate makes vacation AC settings fundamentally different from the rest of the country. While homeowners in Atlanta or Dallas can safely set their thermostats to 85°F and walk away, South Florida's outdoor dew points regularly hit 75–78°F in July and August. That narrows your margin for error to nearly zero.

## The Problem with "Turning It Off"

It's tempting to flip the AC system to OFF before a two-week vacation. After all, nobody's home to enjoy the cool air, right?

**Wrong.** In South Florida, turning your AC off completely during summer is one of the fastest ways to destroy your home's indoor environment and your HVAC equipment.

Here's what happens inside a closed South Florida home with no AC for 48+ hours:

1. **Indoor relative humidity spikes to 80–90%** within 12–24 hours (outdoor moisture infiltrates through cracks, open ductwork, and the slab)
2. **Condensation forms on every cool surface** — drywall, furniture, hardwood floors, interior doors
3. **Mold spores germinate in 24–48 hours** once humidity exceeds 70%
4. **Wood flooring and cabinetry** begin swelling and delaminating
5. **Electronics and appliances** suffer internal corrosion
6. **The AC system itself** — if it has a flooded evaporator coil from restarting in high-humidity air — can slug refrigerant or damage the compressor

The result? You come home from vacation to a musty smell, visible mold growth, warped floors, and a repair bill that dwarfs the energy savings.

## The Right Temperature: 80–82°F

For a South Florida summer vacation, the sweet spot is **80–82°F** with the thermostat fan set to **AUTO** (never ON).

At 80°F with AUTO fan mode:

- The AC only runs when the temperature exceeds the setpoint — typically 3–5 cycles per day depending on outdoor temps, shade, and insulation quality
- Each cycle removes significant moisture before the blower shuts off, keeping indoor humidity in the 50–60% range
- Energy savings: roughly 25–35% compared to your occupied 74–76°F setpoint
- The system isn't working hard enough to freeze up or short-cycle

**Why not 85°F?** At 85°F in South Florida's July humidity, your indoor RH will drift to 65–75% — within the range where mold growth becomes possible, especially in closets, bathrooms, and corners with poor airflow. The additional 3°F of savings (80→85) isn't worth the mold risk.

**Why not 78°F?** Minimal savings. The compressor still runs frequently, and the small temperature reduction doesn't justify the energy cost for an empty house.

## The Fan Setting: AUTO, Never ON

Setting your thermostat fan to ON (continuous) during vacation is a common mistake made by well-meaning homeowners trying to "keep air moving."

Here's the problem: In ON mode, the blower runs 24/7, but the compressor only runs when cooling is needed. What that means in practice:

- The blower continuously pushes air across the **wet evaporator coil** between cooling cycles
- This re-evaporates the condensate back into your airstream
- Indoor humidity **rises**, not falls
- The system works harder to pull that moisture back out on the next cycle

**AUTO mode** lets the coil drain completely between cycles. The brief off periods between compressor runs give the condensate pan time to empty through the drain line, and the evaporator fins dry out rather than wicking moisture back into the air.

## Smart Thermostat Vacation Features

If you have a smart thermostat (Nest, Ecobee, Honeywell, or similar), use its built-in **vacation mode** rather than manually changing the setpoint:

- **Vacation mode** maintains your chosen temperature while suppressing occupancy-based features (auto-away scheduling, motion sensing, geofencing) that could cause erratic temperature swings
- **Remote monitoring** lets you check temperature and humidity from your phone — invaluable if a thunderstorm knocks out power while you're away
- **Freeze protection** in most smart thermostats prevents pipes from freezing (rare in South Florida but possible during polar vortex dips)
- **High-temperature alerts** notify you if the AC fails mid-vacation

Set a push alert threshold at 88°F — if the indoor temp hits that, your AC has failed or lost power, and you need a neighbor or [emergency AC service](https://ac-repair.today/services/emergency-ac-repair/) to investigate before the humidity damage sets in.

## Pre-Vacation Checklist

Do this 24–48 hours before departure:

- [ ] **Change the air filter** — A clean filter maximizes efficiency and dehumidification. A dirty filter restricts airflow, which causes the coil to run colder (potentially freezing) and reduces moisture removal.
- [ ] **Clear the condensate drain line** — Pour a cup of distilled white vinegar through the access tee or main drain line. South Florida's summer heat accelerates algae growth in drain pans. A clogged drain while you're away = water damage.
- [ ] **Check the condensate pump** (if you have one in an attic unit or basement) — Ensure it's cycling properly. A failed pump will trigger the safety float switch and shut down the entire system.
- [ ] **Inspect the outdoor unit** — Clear vegetation, debris, and tall grass within 24 inches of the condenser cabinet. A [quick AC maintenance check](https://ac-repair.today/services/ac-maintenance/) before vacation can catch loose electrical connections or declining refrigerant charge that would fail under extended runtime.
- [ ] **Close curtains and blinds** — South-facing windows let in enormous heat gain. Blackout curtains or reflective blinds reduce indoor heat load by 15–25%, meaning fewer AC cycles per day.
- [ ] **Check the circuit breaker** — Ensure the AC breaker hasn't tripped during a recent storm. Confirm the disconnect switch at the outdoor unit is fully engaged.
- [ ] **Seal the envelope** — Check that all windows and exterior doors are fully closed and locked. Even a 1/8-inch gap around a sliding glass door leaks conditioned air and lets humid outdoor air infiltrate.
- [ ] **Set the water heater to vacation mode** (if applicable) — Reduces standby heat loss and lowers electric load, but doesn't affect the AC.

## Post-Vacation Return Protocol

When you walk back in after a week or two away, don't immediately crank the thermostat to 72°F. That shocks the system and can freeze the evaporator coil.

**Better approach:**

1. Open all interior doors (closets, bedrooms, bathrooms) to equalize temperature across zones
2. Set the thermostat to 78°F on AUTO and let it run for 2–3 hours
3. After the initial pull-down, lower to your normal occupied setpoint in 2°F increments every 60 minutes
4. Check for musty odors — if you smell mildew in specific rooms, inspect closets and corners (the most common mold growth spots during vacancy)
5. Check the condensate drain pan for standing water or algae buildup
6. Run ceiling fans on medium to help distribute cooled air during the recovery period

## What About a Mid-Vacation Thunderstorm Power Outage?

South Florida summer thunderstorms are a near-daily occurrence, and losing power for 2–6 hours is common even in good weather. When the power returns:

- Your thermostat will resume the vacation setpoint automatically
- The AC's built-in **short-cycle protection** (typically 3–5 minutes) prevents the compressor from restarting against high head pressure
- If you have a smart thermostat with outage detection, you'll get a notification — check the indoor temperature 2 hours after power restoration to confirm the AC is cooling properly

If power is out for more than 8 hours during a vacation gap, the indoor humidity can spike regardless of your AC settings. This is where having a neighbor or property manager check on the home becomes important. For frequent travelers, a **smart humidity monitor** (like Aqara, Govee, or SensorPush) paired with your phone provides peace of mind — and early warning if the AC fails mid-trip.

## Bottom Line

| Setting | Recommend? | Result |
|---------|-----------|--------|
| OFF | ❌ | Mold, humidity damage, compressor slugging risk |
| 85°F AUTO | ⚠️ | Marginal — 65–75% RH, mold risk in problem areas |
| 80–82°F AUTO | ✅ | 50–60% RH, 25–35% energy savings, safe return |
| 78°F AUTO | ❌ | Minimal savings, doesn't justify the energy cost |
| Any temp, fan ON | ❌ | Re-evaporates moisture, raises indoor humidity |

Set it to **80–82°F, fan AUTO**, change the filter, clear the drain, and enjoy your vacation knowing your AC isn't working harder than it needs to — and your home will be comfortable and mold-free when you return.
