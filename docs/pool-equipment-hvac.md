# AC for Pool Equipment Rooms & Outdoor Structures

Pool equipment rooms, pump houses, and garden sheds that house pool heaters, pumps, and filtration systems present unique HVAC challenges in South Florida. Unlike conditioned living spaces, these rooms typically function as semi-outdoor enclosures where humidity, salt air, and heat from mechanical equipment create a corrosive microclimate that can destroy electronics, shorten equipment lifespan, and grow mold.

This guide covers how South Florida homeowners can protect both their pool equipment and the building it lives in.

## Why Pool Equipment Rooms Are Different

A typical South Florida pool equipment enclosure is a small room (4×6 to 8×10 ft) containing:

- **Pool pump and motor** — generates heat and vibration
- **Pool heater** (gas or heat pump) — combustion exhaust clearance needed
- **Salt chlorinator or chemical feeder** — corrosive chemical vapors
- **Control panels and timers** — sensitive electronics
- **Plumbing manifolds** — potential leak sources

Without proper ventilation and temperature management, this room becomes:

- **10–20°F hotter** than outside air during summer (equipment heat load)
- **Near 100% humidity** from standing water and condensate
- **Salt-laden air** accelerates corrosion on every exposed metal surface (within 5 miles of the coast, doubly so near pools with salt chlorinators)

The result: pump motors fail 2–3× faster, control board corrosion causes intermittent faults, and wood-framed enclosures develop rot in 3–5 years.

## Ventilation Requirements

### Passive Ventilation (Minimum)

At minimum, every pool equipment enclosure needs:

- **Low vents** (intake) near the floor — at least 144 sq in per 1,000 CFM of equipment
- **High vents** (exhaust) near the ceiling — same area, opposite wall for cross-flow
- **Louvered or screened openings** — keeps wildlife out while moving air
- **No direct soffit venting** — warm moist air rises into rafters → mold

Check vents quarterly — South Florida rain and landscaping debris clog screened louvers surprisingly fast. Palm fronds, blown mulch, and spider webs are the top three offenders.

### Active Ventilation (Recommended)

For rooms with large equipment (pool heaters over 200K BTU, or heat pumps), active ventilation is the difference between 15-year and 5-year equipment life:

- **Wall-mounted exhaust fan** with thermostat + humidistat control
  - Minimum: 6 air changes per hour
  - Typical: 200–400 CFM for a standard 6×8×8 room
- **Intake grille** on the opposite wall, sized 20% larger than the fan opening
- **Activation thresholds**: fan triggers at 95°F or 70% relative humidity
- **Run time**: continuous during pool season (March–November in South Florida)

### Gas Heater Combustion Air

If a gas pool heater shares the enclosure, combustion air rules apply:

- **Indoor gas heater**: requires two permanent openings — within 12 inches of the floor and 12 inches of the ceiling, each at least 100 sq in for most residential heaters
- **Manufacturer clearances**: minimum 12–24 inches from all combustible materials (check your model — this varies widely)

**Safety note**: Carbon monoxide from backdrafting gas pool heaters is a real but rarely discussed risk in tight enclosures. Install a CO alarm in any room with a gas heater. It costs $25. It has saved lives in South Florida.

## Salt-Air Corrosion Protection

Salt air is the single biggest equipment killer in coastal South Florida pool equipment rooms. Unlike general coastal corrosion, pool equipment rooms concentrate salt via evaporation from chlorinated water puddles, salt-system backwash, and high humidity that keeps salt particles suspended.

### What Gets Damaged

| Component | Failure Mode | Typical Life Without Protection |
|-----------|-------------|-------------------------------|
| Pump motor bearings | Rust seizes bearings | 3–5 years (1–2 years within 1 mile of coast with pool) |
| Control board contacts | Copper corrosion → intermittent failures | 2–4 years |
| Heater heat exchanger | Chloride pitting | 5–8 years |
| Timer mechanisms | Internal gear corrosion | 2–3 years |
| Electrical disconnects | Contactor welding | 3–5 years |

### Protection Strategies

1. **Ventilate aggressively** — every percentage point of humidity reduction cuts corrosion rate measurably. Target below 60% RH.

2. **Coating electronics**: Spray exposed circuit boards with **conformal coating** (MG Chemicals 422B or equivalent) — a $15 can protects control panels for years. This is the single highest-ROI action.

3. **NEMA 4X enclosures**: When replacing disconnects or control boxes, use NEMA 4X-rated (fiberglass or stainless steel) instead of standard NEMA 1 or 3R. The upcharge is ~$40–$80 but doubles lifespan.

4. **Sacrificial zinc anodes**: In extreme environments, small zinc anodes mounted inside the enclosure can be grounded to reduce galvanic corrosion. More common for mechanics than homeowners, but worth knowing about.

5. **Annual contact cleaning**: Turn off power, remove panels, and spray electrical contacts with **CRC QD Electronic Cleaner** (or DeoxIT), then apply a protective coating. This 15-minute annual task prevents most electrical-based "ghost failures."

## Condensate Management

Pool equipment rooms generate condensate from three sources:

- **AC condensate drains** routed through the room
- **Pool heater condensate** (high-efficiency gas heaters produce acidic condensate)
- **Surface condensation** on cold pipes in humid air

### Problems

Condensate that pools on the floor:
- Raises humidity toward 100% (even with ventilation)
- Creates mold growth on drywall and wood
- Rusts equipment feet and mounting brackets
- Attracts insects (cockroaches and ants love warm, damp floor corners)

### Solutions

- **Drain all condensate into a dedicated floor drain or condensate pump** — never let it evaporate into the room
- **Insulate cold water pipes** that run through the enclosure (3/8-in foam pipe insulation, minimum)
- **Slope the floor** toward a drain (or install a floor trench drain during the next remodel)
- **Use a condensate neutralizer** for high-efficiency gas heater condensate — the acidic output (pH 3–5) eats concrete and copper over time

## Temperature Management

Equipment room internal heat load comes from:
- **Pool pump motors**: 1–2 HP motors dump 750–1500 watts of heat into the room during operation
- **Heat pump pool heaters**: adds another 500–1500 W
- **Transformer heat**: from control voltage transformers

Without ventilation, internal temperatures regularly hit 115–130°F. At these temperatures:

- Motor winding insulation degrades twice as fast per 18°F above rated temp
- Capacitors fail prematurely (electrolyte dries out faster)
- Electronic controls enter thermal shutdown

### Signs Your Equipment Room Overheats

- You can feel a strong heat wave when opening the door
- Pool pump thermal overload trips on hot days
- Timer displays fading or blank (LCD heat damage)
- You find yourself replacing the same pump every 2 years

## DIY Checklist

Every South Florida homeowner with a pool equipment enclosure should do this twice a year (before summer and before winter):

- [ ] Clear louvers and vents of debris, spider webs, and nesting
- [ ] Test exhaust fan operation (if equipped) — hold tissue to intake grille
- [ ] Spray circuit boards with anti-corrosion coating
- [ ] Flush condensate drain line with vinegar
- [ ] Insulate any bare cold-water pipes
- [ ] Check heater combustion air openings for blockage
- [ ] Test CO alarm (if gas heater present)
- [ ] Clean pump motor cooling fins (vacuum dust buildup)
- [ ] Check floor for standing water or persistent damp spots

## When to Call a Professional

Call a licensed HVAC contractor if:

- You need to add active ventilation (exhaust fan) to the enclosure
- Condensate from AC equipment backs up into the pool room

- Rust or corrosion has already caused pump or control failures — you may need an equipment assessment
