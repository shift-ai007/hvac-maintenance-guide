# Condensate Drain Maintenance Guide

Your AC system removes gallons of water from your home's air every day. In South Florida, where relative humidity rarely drops below 60%, a typical 3-ton AC unit pulls **10-20 gallons of condensate per day** during summer. All that water has to go somewhere — and when the drain line clogs, it ends up in your ceiling, walls, or floors.

## Why South Florida Homes Are at Higher Risk

Three environmental factors make condensate drain clogs the single most common AC service call in our region:

| Factor | Impact |
|--------|--------|
| **Constant humidity** | AC runs 2,000+ hours/year producing condensate. More runtime = faster biofilm growth |
| **Warm attic temperatures** | Drain lines in unconditioned attics stay warm, accelerating algae and mold growth inside the PVC |
| **Dust and debris** | Construction dust, drywall particles, and pet dander get pulled through the system and settle in the drain pan |

## How the Condensate Drain System Works

Your AC has two drain lines:

1. **Primary drain** — Carries condensate from the evaporator coil drain pan to a household drain, outside spigot, or plumbing stack. This is the one that clogs.

2. **Secondary/emergency drain** — A backup drain slightly higher on the drain pan. If water reaches this line, it means the primary is clogged. Usually plumbed to drip in an obvious location (above a window, front door, or garage) so you notice it.

3. **Float switch** (if equipped) — A safety device in the drain pan that cuts power to the AC when water rises too high. Not all systems have one — if yours doesn't, you should install one.

## Signs Your Drain Line Is Clogging

Catch it early and it's a $100 service call. Miss it and it's a ceiling replacement:

- **Water pooling around the indoor unit** — The drain pan is overflowing
- **Musty or mildew smell from vents** — Standing water in the pan breeds mold
- **Water dripping from the ceiling near the air handler** — Emergency: primary AND secondary drains are both blocked
- **AC won't turn on** — Float switch cut power (this is a good thing — it saved your ceiling)
- **Gurgling sound from the drain line** — Air is bubbling through sludge in the pipe
- **Secondary drain dripping** — If you see water dripping from a pipe above a window or door, your primary is clogged

## DIY Maintenance: Vinegar Flush

The single most effective preventive measure costs about $3 and takes 10 minutes:

**What you need**: White vinegar, funnel, wet-dry vac (optional)

**Steps**:

1. Turn off the AC at the thermostat and breaker
2. Locate the condensate drain access point — usually a T-fitting with a vertical cap near the indoor unit
3. Remove the cap (it's not glued — just lifts off)
4. Pour 2 cups of white vinegar down the access pipe
5. Let it sit for 30 minutes. The vinegar dissolves algae, kills mold, and loosens the sludge
6. Flush with 2-3 cups of warm water
7. If you have a wet-dry vac, go outside and vacuum the drain line exit point for 30 seconds to pull through any remaining debris
8. Replace the cap and restore power

**Do this every 3 months.** Set a calendar reminder. In South Florida, monthly during June-September peak season.

## When Vinegar Isn't Enough

If the drain hasn't been cleaned in a year or more, vinegar alone may not clear it:

- **Bleach** — Effective but damages PVC over time and the fumes enter your ductwork. Avoid if possible.
- **Compressed air** — Blasting from the inside access point outward can clear solid blockages. Cover the opening with a rag to avoid blowback.
- **Enzymatic drain cleaner** — Products like Nu-Calgon Drain Solve use enzymes rather than harsh chemicals. Safe for PVC and septic systems.
- **Wet-dry vac from the outside** — Creates a strong vacuum that can pull solid clogs free. Duct-tape the hose to the drain exit for a tight seal.

## The Secondary Drain and Float Switch

**Test your float switch** by pouring a small amount of water into the secondary drain opening. If the AC shuts off within a minute, the switch works. If nothing happens, your float switch may be faulty — this is the only thing between you and a flooded ceiling.

**Never cap the secondary drain** — it's there as an overflow indicator. If water drips from it, address the primary clog immediately.

## Professional Cleaning

If you haven't had the drain line professionally cleaned in over a year, call a technician. They have:

- Commercial-grade vacuum pumps that pull 10x the suction of a shop vac
- Inspection cameras to check for PVC separation or cracks inside walls
- Biocide tablets that go in the drain pan and release algae-inhibiting compounds for 3-6 months

## When the Drain Pan Itself Is the Problem

Older systems (10+ years) may have a rusted or cracked drain pan. No amount of drain cleaning fixes a leaking pan — it needs replacement. Signs of a bad pan:

- Rust-colored water stains around the indoor unit
- Water leaking from the bottom of the unit even after drain line cleaning
- Visible rust or cracks when you look inside the access panel

Replacing a drain pan on a 10+ year old system often isn't worth the labor cost. In these cases, the money is better spent toward a new, more efficient unit.

## Prevention Summary

| Frequency | Task |
|-----------|------|
| Monthly (summer) | Vinegar flush |
| Every 3 months (winter) | Vinegar flush |
| Annually | Professional drain line cleaning with inspection camera |
| As needed | Install float switch if not present |
