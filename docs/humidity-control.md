# Humidity Control for South Florida Homes

South Florida's subtropical climate means outdoor humidity regularly exceeds 80%. Your HVAC system is your primary defense against excess indoor moisture, but standard cooling alone often isn't enough. This guide covers how humidity affects your home, what your HVAC system does about it, and when you need additional solutions.

## Why Humidity Matters More Than Temperature

Most homeowners focus on temperature, but humidity is the hidden driver of comfort and health problems in South Florida. At 78°F with 35% humidity, you feel comfortable. At 78°F with 70% humidity, you feel miserable. The difference isn't temperature — it's moisture.

### Health Risks of High Indoor Humidity

| Humidity Level | Risk |
|---|---|
| Above 60% | Dust mites thrive, triggering allergies and asthma |
| Above 65% | Mold begins growing on surfaces within 24-48 hours |
| Above 70% | Bacterial growth accelerates, respiratory issues worsen |
| Above 75% | Structural damage to drywall, wood, and insulation |

The EPA recommends keeping indoor humidity between 30% and 50%. In South Florida, hitting that range requires intentional effort.

### Property Damage From Excess Moisture

Chronic high humidity in South Florida homes causes:

- **Mold growth** behind walls, in closets, and on ceilings — remediation costs $2,000-$15,000
- **Wood rot** in door frames, baseboards, and structural members
- **Paint bubbling and peeling** on interior walls
- **Musty odors** that permeate fabrics, carpets, and furniture
- **Warped hardwood floors** — replacement runs $8-$15 per square foot
- **Corroded electronics and appliances** — moisture accelerates oxidation

## How Your AC System Removes Humidity

Air conditioners don't just cool air — they dehumidify it. As warm, humid air passes over the cold evaporator coil, moisture condenses on the coil surface and drains away through the condensate line. A properly sized and functioning AC system removes 5-20 gallons of water per day from your home.

### Why Your AC Might Not Be Dehumidifying Enough

**Oversized systems** are the most common cause of poor humidity control. An oversized AC cools the air quickly but shuts off before running long enough to pull moisture from the air. Short cycling — turning on and off every few minutes — means the coil never gets cold enough for sustained dehumidification.

Signs your system is oversized:
- The house cools fast but feels clammy
- The AC runs in short bursts (under 10 minutes)
- You see condensation on windows despite running the AC
- Rooms feel cold but uncomfortable

**Other causes of poor dehumidification**:

- **Dirty evaporator coils** reduce condensation surface area
- **Low refrigerant** means the coil doesn't get cold enough
- **Frozen coils** block airflow and stop dehumidification entirely
- **Fan set to ON instead of AUTO** — when the fan runs continuously, moisture on the coil re-evaporates into the air between cooling cycles

### The Fan Switch Rule

Set your thermostat fan to **AUTO**, not **ON**. When set to ON, the blower runs even when the compressor is off. This blows unconditioned air over the wet evaporator coil, re-evaporating the moisture you just removed. Setting it to AUTO ensures the fan only runs during cooling cycles, keeping that moisture in the drain pan where it belongs.

## Whole-House Dehumidifiers

When your AC system alone can't maintain indoor humidity below 55%, a whole-house dehumidifier is the next step. These units integrate with your existing ductwork and pull moisture from the air independently of cooling.

### When You Need One

- Indoor humidity stays above 55% even with AC running
- You live near the coast or the Everglades (higher ambient moisture)
- Your home has a large footprint with poor air sealing
- You keep the AC set above 76°F (less runtime = less dehumidification)
- You notice musty smells, condensation on windows, or visible mold

### Sizing

Whole-house dehumidifiers are rated in pints per day. For South Florida:

| Home Size | Recommended Capacity |
|---|---|
| Under 1,500 sq ft | 70-90 pints/day |
| 1,500-2,500 sq ft | 90-120 pints/day |
| 2,500-3,500 sq ft | 120-150 pints/day |
| Over 3,500 sq ft | 150+ pints/day (or multiple units) |

These ratings assume typical South Florida construction. Older homes with poor insulation or air sealing may need higher capacity.

### Installation

A whole-house dehumidifier connects to your return air duct. A licensed HVAC technician installs a bypass duct that routes air through the dehumidifier and back into the supply side. The unit runs on its own humidistat, independent of the thermostat.

### Cost

- Equipment: $1,800-$3,500 depending on capacity
- Installation: $500-$1,200
- Annual operating cost: $150-$300 in electricity
- Payback: Prevents $2,000-$15,000 in mold remediation and structural repairs

## Portable Dehumidifiers: A Temporary Fix

Portable dehumidifiers work for a single room or as a stopgap. They're not a substitute for whole-house solutions, but they can help in problem areas like closets, spare bedrooms, and garages.

### Limitations

- Only treat one room at a time
- Must be emptied manually (unless plumbed to a drain)
- Noisy — 50-60 dB, comparable to a conversation
- Energy-intensive for the small area they cover
- Don't address the root cause

## Preventing Humidity Problems

### Air Sealing

Every crack, gap, and opening in your home's envelope lets humid outdoor air in. Priority air sealing targets:

- **Windows and doors** — weatherstripping and caulking
- **Recessed lighting** — use IC-rated airtight housings
- **Electrical outlets on exterior walls** — foam gaskets behind cover plates
- **Plumbing and electrical penetrations** — spray foam around pipes and wires
- **Attic access hatches** — weatherstrip and insulate

### Ventilation Strategy

South Florida homes need controlled ventilation — not random air leaks. An ERV (Energy Recovery Ventilator) brings in fresh outdoor air while transferring moisture from the incoming air to the outgoing exhaust. This provides fresh air without the humidity penalty.

### Bathroom and Kitchen Exhaust

Run bathroom exhaust fans for 30 minutes after showers. Use range hoods when cooking. Make sure exhaust ducts terminate outside — not into the attic, which is common in older Florida homes and creates massive moisture problems.

### Condensate Line Maintenance

Your AC's condensate drain removes gallons of water daily. A clogged line causes water to back up, potentially flooding your air handler closet and feeding mold growth. Flush the line with vinegar monthly during cooling season.

## Monitoring Humidity

A $15 digital hygrometer tells you exactly where your home stands. Place one in a central living area and one in the most problematic room (usually a master closet or spare bedroom). Check readings weekly.

Target ranges:
- **30-45%**: Ideal
- **45-55%**: Acceptable, monitor closely
- **Above 55%**: Take action — your home is at risk

## When to Call a Professional

If your home consistently stays above 55% humidity despite running the AC, something needs attention. Common causes include duct leaks pulling humid attic air into the system, refrigerant issues reducing coil temperature, or oversized equipment short-cycling.

A qualified HVAC technician can measure airflow, check refrigerant charge, inspect ductwork, and recommend the right solution — whether that's a repair, a whole-house dehumidifier, or in some cases, properly sizing a replacement system.
