# Hurricane Prep for Your HVAC System

South Florida's hurricane season runs June 1 through November 30, with peak activity in August-October. Your outdoor condenser unit is particularly vulnerable.

## Before Hurricane Season (June)

- **Document your system**: Photograph the data plate on your indoor and outdoor units. Record make, model, serial number, and installation date. Store photos in your phone and cloud storage.
- **Review insurance**: Confirm your homeowner's insurance covers HVAC damage from wind and flood. Note your deductible.
- **Tighten mounting**: Check that condenser unit mounting bolts are secure. Vibration can loosen them over time.
- **Trim trees**: Cut back any branches within 10 feet of your outdoor unit.
- **Know your shutoffs**: Locate your electrical disconnect (usually a gray box near the outdoor unit) and your main breaker panel.

## When a Storm Is Approaching (48-72 hours out)

1. **Turn off the system** at the thermostat
2. **Turn off the electrical disconnect** at the outdoor unit
3. **Turn off the breaker** for the AC at your main panel
4. **Do NOT cover the condenser** with a tarp — trapped moisture causes more damage than wind. The unit is designed to handle rain.
5. **Secure loose objects** near the condenser (patio furniture, potted plants) that could become projectiles
6. **Clear the drain line** one final time — you don't want a backup during the storm
7. If in a flood zone and water could reach the unit, consider disconnecting the refrigerant lines (professional only)

## During the Storm

- Keep the system OFF
- Do not attempt any outdoor checks during the storm
- If you lose power, the system protects itself by staying off

## After the Storm

**Do NOT immediately turn on your AC.** Follow this sequence:

### Step 1: Visual Inspection (outside)
- Check for visible damage to the condenser unit
- Look for standing water around the unit
- Check for debris on or inside the condenser
- Inspect refrigerant lines for damage
- Check electrical disconnect for water intrusion

### Step 2: Clear Debris
- Remove any debris from around and on top of the unit
- Gently rinse the condenser coils with a garden hose
- Clear the condensate drain line

### Step 3: Check for Flooding
If the outdoor unit was submerged (even partially):
- **Do NOT turn it on** — submerged electrical components can short circuit
- **Call a professional** — the unit needs to be inspected, dried, and tested before operation
- Submerged compressors may need oil replacement
- Electrical contacts and relays may need replacement

### Step 4: Restart Sequence
If no flooding or visible damage:
1. Turn on the breaker at the main panel
2. Turn on the electrical disconnect at the outdoor unit
3. Wait 30 minutes (allows pressure to equalize)
4. Set thermostat to COOL, 5 degrees below current indoor temp
5. Listen for normal startup sounds
6. Check that cool air is coming from vents within 5 minutes

### Warning Signs After a Storm
- **Grinding or screeching**: Debris may be inside the unit
- **No response**: Electrical damage, tripped safety switch, or utility power issue
- **Burning smell**: Electrical short — turn off immediately
- **Unit runs but no cooling**: Possible refrigerant leak from debris impact
- **Water leaking inside**: Drain line blocked by storm debris

## Power Outage Recovery

After extended power outages (common after hurricanes):

1. Don't rush to turn on the AC when power returns — the grid is unstable
2. Wait 15-30 minutes after power restoration
3. Set thermostat to a moderate temperature (78F) — don't try to cool a hot house from 95F to 72F instantly
4. Close blinds and unnecessary doors to reduce the cooling load
5. If the system trips the breaker on startup, wait 30 minutes and try once more. If it trips again, call a professional.

---

*After any hurricane, prioritize safety. If you smell gas, hear hissing, or see standing water near electrical equipment, evacuate and call your utility company.*
