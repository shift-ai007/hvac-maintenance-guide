# Seasonal & Vacation Home AC Management Guide

South Florida is home to one of the largest seasonal and snowbird populations in the country. If you own a home here that sits empty for weeks or months at a time — or if you rent your property seasonally — your AC system faces conditions that year-round residents never deal with.

An idle AC in South Florida's humidity is at higher risk of failure than one running daily. This guide covers how to protect your system while you are away and what to do when you return.

## The Core Problem: Humidity + Stillness

South Florida's average outdoor relative humidity runs 70–80% year-round. Inside a closed, unairconditioned home, that number climbs to 85%+ within 48 hours. At those levels:

- **Mold growth** begins on organic surfaces within 24–72 hours
- **Wood warps** — flooring, cabinets, and doors absorb moisture and swell
- **Electronic corrosion** accelerates on thermostat contacts, circuit boards, and wire connections
- **Dust mites** thrive and multiply exponentially
- **Musty odors** develop in fabrics, carpets, and drywall

The goal of vacation-home AC management is **humidity control first, temperature second**.

## Setting Your AC While Away

### The "85°F Rule" Is Wrong

Many seasonal homeowners are told to set their AC to 85°F while away. This is dangerously high for South Florida. At 85°F and 80% humidity, the dew point is 78°F — well above the 55°F needed to prevent mold growth. The compressor will rarely run, and your home becomes a mold incubator.

### Recommended Away Settings

| Duration | Thermostat Setpoint | Fan Setting | Notes |
|----------|-------------------|-------------|-------|
| 1–7 days | 80–82°F | AUTO | Standard energy-saver; fine for short absences |
| 1–4 weeks | 78–80°F | AUTO | Keeps humidity below 60% in most homes |
| 1–3 months | 76–78°F | AUTO | Maintains safe humidity; cost ~$40–60/month extra |
| 3+ months | 74–76°F | AUTO | Full humidity control; protects structure and contents |

The key metric is **indoor relative humidity**, not temperature. Below 60% RH, mold cannot grow. Above 60% RH, you are gambling with your home's interior.

### Use a Thermostat with Remote Monitoring

If your vacation home has a smart thermostat (and it should), set it to send alerts for:
- Temperature above 85°F → AC may have failed
- Humidity above 65% → System is not dehumidifying adequately
- System offline → Power outage or Wi-Fi disconnection

South Florida thunderstorms cause frequent power flickers that can leave a thermostat offline without the homeowner's knowledge. Some smart thermostats offer cellular backup ($5–10/month).

## Pre-Departure Checklist

### 1. Change the Air Filter
Install a fresh MERV-8 filter the day you leave. A dirty filter restricts airflow, causes evaporator coil freeze, and can damage the compressor.

### 2. Clear the Condensate Drain
Pour one cup of white vinegar through the condensate drain port. This kills any biofilm or algae that has started growing in the line. A clogged drain is the number-one cause of water damage claims in seasonal homes.

### 3. Check the Float Switch
Pour a cup of water into the secondary drain pan — the float should rise and shut the system off. Clean a stuck float mechanism with vinegar and a soft brush.

### 4. Inspect Outdoor Unit Clearance
South Florida vegetation grows fast. Trim any plants, shrubs, or weeds within 3 feet of the condenser.

### 5. Seal the Thermostat
Tape a note saying "Do not adjust below 76°F." Guests and property managers often crank the AC to 68°F on arrival, shocking a lightly-running system.

## Returning After an Absence

### Don't Crank It Down Immediately
Rapid cooling of a humid space causes condensation inside walls and ducts. Instead:
1. Open windows for 15–30 minutes to equalize conditions
2. Close windows and set thermostat to 80°F
3. After 2 hours, lower to 78°F
4. After another 2 hours, lower to normal setpoint (74–76°F)

### Check for Problems Before Full Use
- Musty smell → possible mold in ducts or coil
- No cold air → refrigerant may have leaked during idle period
- Loud noise → debris or animals in condenser
- Water stains → clogged condensate drain
- Ice on refrigerant lines → low charge or restricted airflow

> Any of these signs means call for [AC repair](https://ac-repair.today/services/ac-repair/) before full failure.

### First-Run Checklist
| Step | Action |
|------|--------|
| 1 | Set thermostat to COOL, 78°F |
| 2 | Listen for compressor and fan start within 30 seconds |
| 3 | Feel supply vent — air should be cool within 5 minutes |
| 4 | Check outdoor unit — warm air from top |
| 5 | Condensate drain should drip within 15 minutes |
| 6 | Measure temperature split: 16–22°F after 30 minutes |
| 7 | Install fresh air filter |
| 8 | Test each zone if zoned system |

## Rental Property Considerations

- **Between tenants**: Change the filter between every tenant. Short-term rentals load filters faster than permanent homes.
- **Set thermostat default to 78°F**, not a wide range. Guests will test the lower end.
- Have a contract with a local [AC maintenance provider](https://ac-repair.today/services/ac-maintenance/) for between-tenant peak-season inspections.

### Hurricane Season Overlap
If absence overlaps hurricane season (June–November):
- Ensure surge protector is functional
- Confirm insurance covers AC damage from power surges
- Know the post-hurricane restart procedure

## If the System Fails While Away

A failed AC in a vacant home creates a cascade:
- **48 hours**: Humidity above 70%
- **72 hours**: Mold spores germinate
- **1 week**: Visible mold on walls and ducts
- **2 weeks**: Structural damage

If your smart thermostat alerts to high temperature:
1. Contact property manager to check for obvious issues
2. Authorize service immediately — an [emergency AC repair](https://ac-repair.today/services/emergency-ac-repair/) call costs far less than mold remediation
3. Have technician set up a dehumidifier to keep humidity below 55%
