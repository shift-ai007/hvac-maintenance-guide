# Thermostat Calibration & Sensor Placement

A thermostat that reads the wrong temperature can cost you comfort and money. In South Florida's humid climate, even a 2-degree offset can mean your AC runs 30-60 minutes longer per cycle — or short-cycles without removing enough humidity. This guide covers how to check calibration, where sensors work best, and when to call a pro.

## Why Calibration Matters

Your thermostat is the brain of your cooling system. When it reads 78°F but the room is actually 75°F, your AC never hits its target and runs unnecessarily. When it reads 78°F but the room is 81°F, you stay uncomfortable and humidity climbs.

In Florida, the difference between 50% and 60% relative humidity is the difference between "feels fine" and "sticky." An uncalibrated thermostat that lets indoor humidity drift above 55% creates conditions where mold and dust mites thrive.

### Common Symptoms of Poor Calibration

- AC runs longer than expected on mild days
- Some rooms feel colder or warmer than the thermostat reading
- You constantly adjust the set temperature up or down
- High humidity despite the AC running frequently
- Higher electric bills compared to the same month last year

## Types of Thermostat Sensors

### Digital Thermistors (Most Common)

Modern programmable and smart thermostats use solid-state thermistors. These are accurate to ±1°F when new, but can drift over time as components age. Most smart thermostats (Nest, ecobee, Honeywell Home) self-calibrate periodically, but the physical placement still matters.

**Accuracy check**: Place a known-accurate digital thermometer next to the thermostat. Wait 15 minutes with both at the same height. If they differ by more than 2°F, calibration or placement is the issue.

### Mechanical Bi-Metal (Older Thermostats)

Found in homes built before 2000 and some rental properties. A coiled strip expands and contracts with temperature. These drift more over time and should be checked annually.

**Adjustment**: Unscrew the cover and look for a small calibration screw or an adjustable mercury switch (rare now). Turn in tiny increments — 1/8 turn changes the setpoint by about 1°F.

### Remote Sensors (Smart Thermostats)

Ecobee, Nest Temperature Sensor, and Honeywell RedLINK sensors let you place sensors in separate rooms. The thermostat averages readings or uses a prioritized sensor.

**Best uses**:
- Nursery or baby's room
- Master bedroom for overnight comfort
- Sun-facing rooms that heat up first
- Basements or bonus rooms over garages

## Optimal Sensor Placement

### The 5-Foot Rule

Mount the thermostat (or place a remote sensor) at approximately 5 feet above the floor. This is the "breathing zone" — the height where people experience the room's temperature. Placement lower than 4 feet reads cooler (warm air rises) and higher than 6 feet reads warmer.

### Where NOT to Place a Thermostat or Sensor

| Location | Problem |
|----------|---------|
| Direct sunlight | Can read 5-10°F higher than actual room temp |
| Near supply vents | Cold air blowing directly on it forces false short-cycles |
| Near return vents | Room air hasn't mixed yet — reads skewed |
| Above kitchen appliances | Oven/range heat creates false readings |
| Near windows or doors | Drafts trigger unnecessary heating or cooling |
| Behind furniture | Blocked airflow prevents accurate readings |
| In hallways near bathrooms | Steam from showers creates humidity spikes |
| Above electronics (TV, router) | Component heat skews readings by 2-4°F |
| Near lamps or light fixtures | Incandescent/ halogen bulbs generate significant heat |

### Room-by-Room Placement Guide

**Living Room / Great Room**: Interior wall, away from windows. At least 4 feet from any vent. If you have a two-story great room, place sensors at both floor levels — smart thermostats struggle with the temperature differential between floor and ceiling.

**Bedrooms**: On an interior wall opposite the bed. Not above the headboard (body heat and breathing affect the local microclimate). If using a remote sensor, nightstand height works.

**Hallways**: The most common spot for a main thermostat. Good — but only if the hallway isn't a dead-end with no airflow. Hallways near the center of the home give the most representative readings.

## Calibration Methods

### Digital Thermostat Calibration

1. **Check current reading**: Use an accurate reference thermometer (digital kitchen thermometer or HVAC-grade thermometer) placed 3-4 inches from the thermostat at the same height.
2. **Compare after 15 minutes**: With the AC off, let both stabilize.
3. **Apply offset**: Most digital thermostats have a calibration offset setting:
   - Honeywell: Menu → Preferences → Temperature Offset
   - ecobee: Settings → Installation Settings → Thresholds → Temperature Correction
   - Nest: Auto-calibrates — but you can force a calibration by restarting
   - Lennox/ carrier dealer stats: Often require a code from the installation manual
4. **Offset range**: ±5°F is typical. Adjust in 1°F increments, then wait 24 hours before rechecking.

### Smart Thermostat Follow-Room Mode

If you have a smart thermostat with multiple remote sensors (ecobee with SmartSensor, for example):

1. Set the thermostat to use the sensor in the room you occupy most.
2. Enable "follow me" or "occupancy" mode so it tracks you through the home.
3. For overnight, schedule the bedroom sensor to be the priority.
4. Check humidity readings — some smart sensors also track humidity and can trigger dehumidification.

### Mechanical Thermostat Adjustment

1. Remove the cover carefully — some have hidden clips.
2. Locate the heat anticipator (a small adjustable arm near the bi-metal coil).
3. Set the anticipator to match the current draw of your system (stamped on the furnace/air handler data plate, e.g., 0.4A).
4. For the temperature calibration screw: turn clockwise to raise the reading point, counterclockwise to lower it. Test with a reference thermometer.

> **Safety note**: If you see mercury (a small glass vial with silver liquid), stop and call a professional. Mercury thermostats must be handled and disposed of as hazardous waste.

## Florida-Specific Considerations

### High Humidity and Sensor Accuracy

Florida's year-round high humidity (often 70-90% outdoor) causes two problems:

1. **Sensor drift**: Humidity accelerates corrosion on exposed thermistor leads. Sensors in Florida homes drift faster than in dry climates — check calibration every 6 months instead of annually.
2. **Condensation on sensors**: If a cold air supply vent blows directly near the thermostat, condensation can form on the sensor and cause wild readings. Redirect the vent or move the thermostat.

### Attic Heat Radiant Effect

Many Florida homes have thermostats mounted on interior walls that back up to unconditioned attics or garages. The wall cavity can be 20°F warmer than the room — heat conducts through the drywall and skews the thermostat reading. Solutions:

- Install an insulated wall box behind the thermostat
- Use a thermostat with a remote sensor placed on a better wall
- Add foam gasket behind the thermostat faceplate

### Power Outage Recovery

Florida thunderstorms cause frequent brief power outages. Some thermostats lose calibration on hard resets:

- **Battery-backed stats**: Check batteries seasonally; dead batteries = factory reset = lost calibration.
- **Hardwired stats**: Most retain settings through brief outages, but prolonged outages (4+ hours) may drain backup capacitors.

After any power outage longer than 30 minutes, verify the displayed temperature matches a reference thermometer.

## When to Call a Professional

Some calibration issues point to bigger problems:

- **Temperature swings of 4+°F** between cycles suggest the thermostat is in the wrong location entirely — relocation costs $150-300 and is worth it.
- **Calibration offset above 5°F** means something else is wrong — duct leak, bad wiring, or failed thermostat.
- **Thermostat shows "wait" or recovery mode every cycle** — may need a new thermostat or system board.
- **Remote sensors drop connection** — wireless interference or dead batteries, but persistent issues suggest range problems in larger Florida homes.
- **Strange behavior after a storm** — lightning surges can fry thermostat electronics even through surge protectors.

If you have a heat pump system (common in newer Florida construction), thermostat issues are more complex — heat pumps need specific thermostat logic for emergency heat, defrost cycles, and auxiliary heat staging. Let a licensed pro handle heat pump thermostat troubleshooting.

## Seasonal Calibration Checklist

Use this twice a year (spring and fall) to stay ahead of Florida's mild temperature changes:

- [ ] Compare thermostat reading to reference thermometer
- [ ] Check remote sensor batteries
- [ ] Verify no furniture is blocking the thermostat or sensors
- [ ] Dust the thermostat interior (with power off, use a soft brush)
- [ ] Confirm AC cycles match the programmed schedule
- [ ] Check humidity reading against a separate hygrometer
- [ ] Tighten any loose thermostat wire connections at the wall
- [ ] Verify the thermostat level — mercury switches are gravity-sensitive
- [ ] Test emergency heat (heat pumps only) to confirm it fires

## Summary

A properly calibrated thermostat with well-placed sensors is the cheapest way to improve comfort and reduce cooling costs in South Florida. Check calibration every 6 months, keep sensors away from drafts and heat sources, and use remote sensors in the rooms that matter most. When your thermostat reads true, your AC does exactly what it's supposed to — no more, no less.
