# Peak Summer Load: Why Your AC Struggles in June and What to Do About It

Every June, HVAC service companies across South Florida see call volume triple. The most common complaint isn't a refrigerant leak or a failed compressor — it's "my AC just can't keep up."

The real problem is that summer pushes your system to its design limits. Small issues that went unnoticed in the milder months become failures under peak load. Here's what actually happens and how to prevent it.

## The Physics of Summer Heat Load

Your AC is rated to remove a specific number of BTUs per hour. That rating assumes a specific temperature differential between the refrigerant and the outside air.

In March, outdoor temps hover around 80°F — the condenser easily dumps heat. In June, that same condenser faces 94°F ambient air with direct sun on the cabinet. The temperature differential shrinks, compressor discharge pressure rises, and the entire system operates near its thermal ceiling.

The measurable effects:

- **Higher amp draw** — the compressor works harder. Weak capacitors fail under this load.
- **Reduced capacity** — the system delivers fewer BTUs per hour than its rated spec. A 10-15% drop is normal; 25%+ means something is wrong.
- **Longer run cycles** — to maintain setpoint, the system runs more hours per day, accelerating wear on every moving part.

## Three Failure Modes That Emerge in June

### 1. Capacitor Degradation

The single most common June service call is a failed run capacitor. These components store electrical charge to help the compressor and fan motors start. Heat accelerates chemical breakdown inside electrolytic capacitors. A capacitor that tested at 370 µF in April may measure 320 µF in June — enough to cause hard-starting, high amp draw, and eventual compressor trip.

**Prevention:** Have a technician test capacitance with a multimeter during your spring tune-up. Capacitors cost $15-35. A failed one on a 95°F day costs a lot more.

### 2. Airflow Restriction Feedback Loop

South Florida's humidity carries dust and lint onto the evaporator coil surface. When airflow is already reduced by a dirty filter (and most filters in June are overdue for replacement), the coil runs colder than designed. More condensation forms, which traps more debris, creating an insulating mud layer that further reduces heat transfer.

**Prevention:** Switch to monthly filter changes during June-August. The 90-day filter schedule that works in other climates fails here.

### 3. Marginal Refrigerant Charge

Small leaks — ounces per year — become symptomatic under peak load. A system 5% low on charge may cool fine at 80°F but fail to reach setpoint at 95°F. Low suction pressure causes the evaporator to ice over, airflow stops, and the system short-cycles on the freeze protection thermostat.

**Prevention:** Have a technician measure superheat at the evaporator outlet and subcooling at the condenser liquid line during your annual inspection. These readings, cross-referenced against the manufacturer's charging chart, reveal the full picture.

## The Three-Tier Response

| Tier | Action | When |
|------|--------|------|
| **1 — Free** | Replace filter, clear condenser debris, listen for odd sounds from the blower | Any time, no tools needed |
| **2 — Professional** | Measure temp drop across evaporator (20°F is ideal), check capacitor health, verify refrigerant pressures | Annually, or when Tier 1 doesn't resolve the issue |
| **3 — Emergency** | Turn system off and call for service | System stopped cooling, loud mechanical noise, breaker won't reset |

## What the Data Says

Systems that survive summer without a breakdown enter June with clean coils, strong capacitors, correct charge, and unrestricted airflow. Systems running at 80% of peak condition because of accumulated neglect cross the failure threshold the first time outdoor temps hit 95°F.

The difference between the two outcomes is a $30 filter subscription and one annual service visit. Over a 10-year system life, that's about $3,000 in maintenance — and it prevents at least one $300-800 emergency repair while keeping energy bills 15-25% lower.
