# When to Replace Your AC System

Replacing an AC system costs $5,000–$15,000. Repairing it costs $150–$2,500. Making the right call depends on age, repair cost, efficiency, and how much longer the old system can realistically last.

## The 50% Rule

If a single repair costs more than 50% of what a new system would cost, replacement makes more financial sense. This is especially true for systems over 10 years old where additional breakdowns are likely within 2–3 years.

**Example**: Your 13-year-old 3-ton system needs a new compressor. Repair: $2,500. New system: $7,000. The repair is 36% of replacement cost — borderline. But at 13 years old with declining efficiency, the next major failure is probably 1–2 years away.

## Age-Based Guidelines

| System Age | Typical Efficiency | Likely Condition | Recommendation |
|---|---|---|---|
| Under 8 years | 14–21 SEER2 | Under warranty or recently warranted | Repair (unless compressor failure) |
| 8–12 years | 13–16 SEER | Parts getting expensive, efficiency declining | Repair if under $1,500; evaluate if more |
| 13–15 years | 10–14 SEER | Near end of expected lifespan | Replace — efficiency gains alone often justify the cost |
| 16+ years | 8–12 SEER | Beyond designed lifespan | Replace as soon as practical |

## The Efficiency Math

In South Florida, an efficiency upgrade pays for itself faster than anywhere else in the country because you run the AC 10–12 months per year.

**Monthly cooling cost comparison** (2,000 sq ft home, $0.13/kWh FPL rate):

| System Rating | Est. Monthly Cooling Cost | Annual vs 10 SEER Savings |
|---|---|---|
| 10 SEER (pre-2006) | $195 | — |
| 14 SEER (2006–2014 standard) | $139 | $672 |
| 15 SEER2 (current minimum) | $130 | $780 |
| 18 SEER2 (mid-range) | $108 | $1,044 |
| 21+ SEER2 (premium) | $93 | $1,224 |

Replacing a 10 SEER system with a 15 SEER2 unit saves ~$780/year. On a $7,000 investment, that's a 5-year payback — then pure savings for the remaining 15+ years of the new system's life.

## Warning Signs

Beyond age and repair costs, these symptoms signal declining performance:

- **Rising bills at the same usage** — The system is losing efficiency. Check by comparing year-over-year bills for the same months
- **Frequent repairs** — More than two service calls per year means cascading failures. Today's fix stresses another component
- **R-22 refrigerant** — If your system uses R-22 (Freon), which was phased out in 2020, a refrigerant recharge now costs $75–$150/lb. A full charge on a residential system can be $800–$1,500. This cost alone justifies replacement
- **Uneven cooling** — Rooms that used to be comfortable aren't anymore. The compressor is losing capacity
- **High indoor humidity** — The system can't dehumidify at reduced capacity. You end up setting the thermostat lower, wasting more energy
- **Noise** — Grinding, squealing, or rattling from the compressor or air handler suggests bearing failure or internal damage

## Choosing a Replacement

### System Types

| Type | Best For | Tradeoff |
|---|---|---|
| Single-stage | Budget-conscious, smaller homes | Least expensive but least efficient at part-load |
| Two-stage | Better comfort, most homes | 15–20% more upfront, better humidity control |
| Variable-speed (inverter) | Maximum efficiency, larger homes | 30–50% more upfront, best long-term savings |

### Sizing

The most common mistake: installing a system that's too large. An oversized AC:
- Short-cycles (runs briefly, shuts off, restarts)
- Doesn't dehumidify properly
- Wears out faster
- Costs more upfront and in operation

**Insist on a Manual J load calculation.** This considers your home's actual characteristics — not just square footage. Reject any contractor who sizes based on a "rule of thumb."

### What to Ask Contractors

When getting quotes, ask:
1. Did you do a Manual J calculation? (If no, find another contractor)
2. What SEER2 rating is this system?
3. What's included — labor, equipment, permits, disposal of old system?
4. What warranty does the manufacturer offer? What do you add?
5. Are there current rebates from FPL or the manufacturer?

Get at least two quotes from licensed contractors. Verify licenses at [myfloridalicense.com](https://www.myfloridalicense.com).

## Rebates and Tax Credits

Stacking available incentives can reduce your out-of-pocket cost significantly:

- **FPL rebates**: Up to $500 for qualifying high-efficiency systems
- **Federal tax credits**: 30% of cost (up to $2,000) for heat pump systems through 2032
- **Manufacturer promotions**: Seasonal rebates of $300–$1,000

Example: An $9,000 18 SEER2 heat pump system → $500 FPL + $2,000 tax credit + $500 manufacturer = **$6,000 net cost**.

Check current incentive availability before purchasing — programs change frequently.
