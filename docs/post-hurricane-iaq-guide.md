# Post-Hurricane Indoor Air Quality: Managing Mold, Humidity, and Airborne Contaminants After a South Florida Storm

When a hurricane passes through South Florida, the immediate risks are obvious — wind damage, flooding, power outages. But the weeks that follow bring a quieter, less visible threat: degraded indoor air quality that can persist for months if not handled correctly.

Post-hurricane IAQ problems stem from three converging factors: **elevated humidity from the storm itself**, **mold growth in water-damaged materials**, and **particulates introduced during cleanup and reconstruction**. Understanding each factor helps homeowners make smart decisions during the recovery window.

## The Humidity Cascade

South Florida's outdoor humidity typically runs 70-90% during hurricane season. After a storm, three things happen that drive indoor humidity even higher:

1. **Power outages disable dehumidification** — your AC is the primary dehumidifier in a Florida home. Without it running, indoor relative humidity can hit 80%+ within hours.
2. **Floodwater and rainwater intrusion** adds moisture to building materials (drywall, wood framing, insulation) that then off-gas into the indoor air.
3. **Reduced ventilation** — post-storm, homes are often sealed up (boarding damaged windows, keeping doors closed for security) which traps moisture inside.

At sustained RH above 60%, dust mites and mold spores begin proliferating. Above 70%, visible mold growth can start within 24-48 hours on porous surfaces.

### Measuring What Matters

After a storm, don't rely on comfort alone. A $15 digital hygrometer tells you your actual RH. If it reads above 60% and you have power, run the AC continuously — even if it means setting the thermostat lower than usual. The dehumidification cycle matters more than the temperature setpoint right now.

If the AC isn't running (no power or damaged system), the priority is **ventilation drying** — open windows and doors on opposite sides of the home to create cross-flow, but only during daylight hours when outdoor RH is at its lowest. At night, close up — outdoor humidity often spikes to near 100% after a storm.

## Mold: The Two-Week Window

Mold remediation experts talk about the **48-hour rule** and the **two-week boundary**:

- **0-48 hours**: If you can dry water-damaged materials within 48 hours, mold growth is unlikely. This is the ideal window.
- **48 hours - 14 days**: Porous materials (drywall, carpet, upholstery) that stay wet this long will almost certainly develop visible mold. Non-porous surfaces (tile, metal, glass) can be cleaned.
- **Beyond 14 days**: By this point, mold has likely colonized wall cavities and HVAC ductwork. Professional remediation is strongly recommended.

### What to Watch For

Not all mold is visible. After a storm, check for these signs:

- **Musty or earthy smell** — the most reliable early indicator, often detectable before visible growth
- **Discoloration on drywall** — especially near baseboards where floodwater wicked up
- **Warped or bubbling paint** — moisture trapped behind the paint layer
- **Condensation on windows or ductwork** — indicates the space isn't drying properly

### The AC System as Mold Vector

Your HVAC system can become a mold distribution network. If mold grows in the air handler, evaporator coil drain pan, or ductwork, every cycle of the fan blows spores throughout the house.

After any flooding or extended power outage (where the system sat off for days in high humidity), have the evaporator coil and drain pan inspected before running the system continuously. A quick visual check of the drain pan and coil surface tells the story — if there's standing water or visible growth, the system needs professional cleaning before it can safely run.

## Particulate Loads During Recovery

The cleanup phase introduces its own air quality hazards:

- **Drywall dust** from cutting out water-damaged sections contains silica and can irritate lungs
- **Sanding and demolition particles** stay airborne for hours
- **Mold spores** become aerosolized during removal
- **Generator exhaust** (CO) is a lethal but odorless risk if generators run too close to intake vents

### Practical Protection

- Run your AC fan continuously (not auto mode) during active cleanup — the filter captures airborne particles
- Upgrade to a MERV 11 or MERV 13 filter during the recovery period — standard MERV 8 lets too much through
- Change filters every 2-3 days during active demolition, then weekly until things settle
- If running a generator, place it at least 20 feet from any open window, door, or fresh air intake

## When to Call a Professional

Post-storm IAQ issues that warrant professional assessment:

- Persistent musty smell after drying is complete
- Visible mold covering more than 10 square feet (EPA guideline for professional remediation)
- Anyone in the home with asthma or respiratory conditions experiencing worsened symptoms
- HVAC ductwork that was submerged or shows visible mold inside registers
- Indoor RH that won't drop below 60% despite running the AC

A licensed HVAC contractor can perform a duct inspection, measure static pressure and airflow, and clean the evaporator coil and drain pan. For ductwork contamination, a NADCA-certified duct cleaning is the appropriate service.

## The Takeaway

Post-hurricane IAQ is manageable with the right sequence: **dry fast, monitor humidity, protect the AC system, upgrade filtration during cleanup.** The systems that cause problems are the same systems that fix them — your AC is your best dehumidifier, air cleaner, and ventilation tool. Keep it running (safely), keep the filters fresh, and watch the humidity gauge, not just the thermostat.
