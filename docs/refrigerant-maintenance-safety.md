# Refrigerant: What Homeowners Should Know

Refrigerant is the lifeblood of your air conditioning system — the chemical compound that absorbs heat from inside your home and releases it outside. While homeowners should never handle refrigerant themselves (it requires EPA Section 608 certification), understanding the basics helps you spot problems early and communicate effectively with service technicians.

## How Refrigerant Works

Your AC system relies on a sealed loop of refrigerant that cycles through four stages:

```
Compressor (high-pressure gas)
    ↓
Condenser coil (gas → liquid, heat released outside)
    ↓
Expansion valve (pressure drops, liquid cools rapidly)
    ↓
Evaporator coil (liquid → gas, heat absorbed from indoor air)
    ↓
(Returns to compressor)
```

When any part of this sealed loop is compromised, cooling performance drops — and your energy bill climbs.

## Common Refrigerant Problems in South Florida

### Slow Leaks

South Florida's salt air accelerates corrosion on condenser coils, which is the most common source of slow refrigerant leaks. A system with a slow leak may:

- Cool adequately for weeks or months then suddenly stop
- Show ice buildup on the larger copper line (suction line) at the outdoor unit
- Run continuously without reaching the set temperature
- Cost 20-40% more to operate while losing refrigerant

**What to do**: If you notice ice on the copper lines at the outdoor unit, turn the system off immediately and [schedule a professional AC maintenance inspection](https://ac-repair.today/services/ac-maintenance/). Running a system with low refrigerant damages the compressor.

### Rapid Leaks (Puncture or Impact)

A sudden loss of cooling with a hissing sound or oil stain near the outdoor unit indicates a puncture — often from landscaping tools, hurricane debris, or a failed brazed joint. In South Florida, post-hurricane refrigerant loss is common when debris strikes outdoor condenser units.

**What to do**: Turn the system off at the breaker (to prevent compressor damage) and call for [emergency AC repair](https://ac-repair.today/services/emergency-ac-repair/) immediately. Do not attempt to patch the line yourself.

## Refrigerant Types Timeline

Understanding the refrigerant in your system helps with repair decisions:

| Refrigerant | Used In | Status | Notes |
|-------------|---------|--------|-------|
| R-22 (Freon) | Pre-2010 systems | Being phased out | $100-200/lb if available; consider replacement |
| R-410A (Puron) | 2010-2024 systems | Current standard | Widely available, EPA phase-down underway |
| R-454B / R-32 | 2025+ new systems | New standard | Lower GWP; not backward compatible with R-410A |
| R-290 (Propane) | Select new mini-splits | Limited use | Flammable; specialized equipment required |

**Key insight for South Florida homeowners**: If your system uses R-22 and develops a leak, the economics now favor [replacement over repair](https://ac-repair.today/services/ac-replacement/) — especially for units over 12 years old.

## Measuring Refrigerant Health

Technicians use two key measurements to assess refrigerant:

### Superheat (fixed orifice systems)
Measures how much the refrigerant gas warms above its boiling point after leaving the evaporator. Too high = low refrigerant (undercharge). Too low = too much refrigerant (overcharge).

### Subcooling (TXV/EXV systems)
Measures how much the liquid refrigerant cools below its condensation point leaving the condenser. Guides proper charge diagnosis on modern systems.

**Homeowner's simple rule**: If the AC is blowing somewhat cool but not cold, and the outdoor unit's larger copper line feels warm instead of cool, suspect a refrigerant issue. Call a licensed [AC repair professional](https://ac-repair.today/services/ac-repair/) for a diagnosis.

## The SEER2-Refrigerant Connection

Higher SEER2 systems (15+ SEER2) operate with different refrigerant pressures and expansion devices than older systems. This means:

- **Mixing refrigerants destroys performance** — putting R-22 in an R-410A system damages seals immediately
- **Refrigerant charge must be exact** — newer systems with electronic expansion valves (EEV) are less forgiving of even minor over/undercharge
- **Vacuum procedure matters** — a proper deep vacuum (below 500 microns) removes moisture and non-condensables. Without it, refrigerant performance degrades rapidly

## Routine Maintenance That Protects Refrigerant Charge

While you can't "top off" refrigerant yourself, good maintenance habits prevent leaks:

1. **Keep the outdoor unit clean** — Rinse condenser coils twice per year (spring and fall) with a garden hose. Remove leaves, grass, and debris within 2 feet of the unit. Salt-accelerated corrosion is a major leak cause in coastal South Florida.

2. **Protect the unit from physical damage** — Install protective bollards or a cage around condensers in areas where lawn equipment or vehicles pass. Even a minor dent in a coil can become a slow leak point.

3. **Monitor performance annually** — Schedule a spring [professional maintenance tune-up](https://ac-repair.today/services/ac-maintenance/) that includes checking refrigerant pressure, superheat/subcooling, and searching for micro-leaks.

4. **Know your refrigerant type** — Ask your technician which refrigerant your system uses and write it down. This information is critical for fast, accurate service when something fails on a weekend or holiday.

## The Bottom Line

Refrigerant is not a "consumable" like oil in a car. A properly installed, leak-free AC system should never need refrigerant added. If your system needs a "refrigerant top-off," you have a leak that must be found and repaired — or the component must be replaced. Paying for partial refrigerant fills is throwing money away. Insist on leak repair or [system replacement](https://ac-repair.today/services/ac-replacement/) as the permanent solution.
