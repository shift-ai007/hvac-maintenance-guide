# Frozen Evaporator Coil — Causes, Diagnosis, and Prevention

Finding ice on your AC system in the middle of a South Florida summer seems backward — but frozen evaporator coils are one of the most common service calls during the hottest months. This guide explains why it happens, how to diagnose the root cause, and what to do about it.

## Quick Summary

| Question | Answer |
|----------|--------|
| **Can I just let it thaw and run it again?** | Yes — but only if you fix the **root cause**. It will freeze again. |
| **Is it safe to run with ice?** | No. Running with a frozen coil can destroy the compressor. |
| **What causes it?** | Almost always: low airflow or low refrigerant |
| **Do I need a professional?** | If airflow fixes don't work, yes — a refrigerant leak requires a licensed technician |
| **Emergency?** | Turn off the AC immediately if you see ice. Run the fan only to speed thawing. |

## Why Coils Freeze in 95°F South Florida Weather

A properly operating evaporator coil runs at approximately 40-45°F — cold enough to condense moisture out of the air, but above freezing. Ice forms when the coil temperature drops **below 32°F**.

This happens through one of two mechanisms:

### 1. Low Airflow (60% of frozen coil calls in South Florida)

The evaporator coil absorbs heat from the air passing over it. When airflow is restricted, the refrigerant absorbs less heat than expected. The refrigerant exits the metering device at its normal cold temperature (about 40°F), but without enough warm air passing over the coil to transfer heat into the refrigerant, the coil temperature drops below freezing.

**Common airflow restrictions in South Florida homes:**
- **Dirty filter** (most common cause — a clean filter costs $5, a frozen coil repair costs $300+)
- **Closed or blocked supply registers** (bedrooms where the door stays closed with register wide open)
- **Dirty evaporator coil** (from years of unfiltered bypass air)
- **Collapsed or crushed flex duct** (especially common in attics where ducts are walked on during roofing work)
- **Undersized return duct** (common in track homes where the return was barely sized for the original 10 SEER unit now supporting a 16 SEER unit needing more airflow)
- **Blower motor running slow** (capacitor failing, motor bearings worn, or ECM module failing)

### 2. Low Refrigerant Charge (35% of frozen coil calls)

A refrigerant leak reduces the pressure in the evaporator coil, which lowers its temperature. In severe cases, the coil can drop to 20°F or below — well into ice territory.

**Key distinction**: With low refrigerant, the ice is often concentrated near the coil's metering device (TXV or piston) and gradually extends outward. With airflow problems, the ice is more uniform across the entire coil face.

### 3. Other Causes (5%)

- **Metering device failure** (TXV stuck open or closed)
- **Oversized system** (cools too quickly without running long enough to dehumidify — can lead to short-cycling that causes freeze-thaw cycles)
- **Low outdoor temperature** (rare in South Florida, but can happen during overnight cold fronts when the system lacks a low-ambient kit)

## How to Diagnose the Root Cause

### Step 1: Identify the Ice Pattern

| Ice Pattern | Most Likely Cause |
|-------------|-------------------|
| Ice at coil inlet (near TXV), extending outward | Low refrigerant charge |
| Ice across entire coil face uniformly | Low airflow |
| Ice only at the bottom of the coil | Dirty coil or partially blocked drain pan |
| Ice reaching back into the suction line | Severe refrigerant floodback (compressor at risk) |
| Ice on outdoor unit in winter | Normal for heat pumps in defrost — not a problem |

### Step 2: Check the Filter First

Stop. Before anything else — **check and replace the air filter**. In a 2023 survey of 500 South Florida AC service calls, the number one found cause was a filter that hadn't been changed in 4+ months. If the filter is clean, check registers and returns.

### Step 3: Check for Airflow

- **Supply register test**: With the system running, place your hand at a supply register. Weak airflow (barely noticeable breeze) suggests a restriction or blower issue.
- **Filter pressure drop**: If the filter looks clean but the system airflow is weak, try running the system with the filter removed for 30 seconds. If airflow dramatically improves, the filter is more restrictive than it looks (common with high-MERV filters).
- **Return grille check**: A return grille that whistles or feels like it's pulling hard means the return path is undersized or restricted.
- **Visual duct check** (attic systems): Look for crushed flex duct, disconnected return drops, or ductwork compressed under stored boxes.

### Step 4: Check the Blower

- **Listen**: Is the blower running? If you hear the outdoor unit running but barely any air from registers, the blower may not be running.
- **Panel access**: Remove the indoor unit panel. Is the blower wheel spinning freely? Is it caked with dust and lint? A dirty blower wheel can reduce airflow by 20-40%.
- **Capacitor check**: If the blower motor hums but doesn't start, the run capacitor is likely dead. This is a $15-30 part.

### Step 5: Rule Out Refrigerant Issues

If airflow checks out and the system still freezes, the problem is likely low refrigerant. **This requires a professional** — refrigerant handling requires EPA 608 certification in the US.

- **Suction line check**: The large (suction) line at the outdoor unit should feel cold and sweaty (40-55°F). If it's barely cool or ambient temperature, charge may be low.
- **Liquid line check**: The small (liquid) line should feel warm (90-110°F). If it's cold or sweating, the system likely has a severe restriction or is critically low on charge.
- **Temperature split**: Measure the temperature at the return grill and the nearest supply register. A split of 14-22°F in cooling mode is normal. Below 14°F suggests low charge or airflow. Above 22°F may indicate low airflow or a restriction.

## What to Do When You Discover a Frozen Coil

### Emergency Steps

1. **Turn the AC (cooling) OFF at the thermostat** — do NOT turn the system off completely. Switch to **FAN ON** mode. This runs the blower continuously, which pulls warm air across the frozen coil, thawing it faster.
2. **Wait for complete thawing**: This takes 2-6 hours depending on ice thickness. Water will drip from the coil into the drain pan — this is normal. If the drain pan overflows, have towels ready.
3. **Check the drain line**: While thawing, verify the condensate drain isn't clogged. A blocked drain will overflow during thawing.
4. **Change the filter**: Regardless of the cause, install a fresh filter before attempting a restart.
5. **Restart cooling**: After the coil is fully thawed (no visible ice, no water from drain), switch the thermostat back to COOL mode.
6. **Monitor for 30 minutes**: If the system freezes again within 30 minutes, the root cause is active — call a professional.

### Why Running With a Frozen Coil Damages the Compressor

When liquid refrigerant returns to the compressor (liquid slugging), it washes out the oil film on the compressor bearings. The compressor runs hotter, lubricates poorly, and eventually seizes. A frozen coil is a step in that direction — the ice insulates the coil, preventing proper heat absorption, and liquid refrigerant can make its way to the compressor.

**Cost of ignoring**: If you run the system with a frozen coil repeatedly, expect compressor failure within 1-2 seasons. Compressor replacement: $1,500-$3,000. New system: $4,500-$9,000.

## When to Call a Professional

**Call immediately if:**
- The system freezes again within 24 hours after a thorough thaw and filter change
- The outdoor unit is running but the blower in the air handler is not
- You suspect a refrigerant leak (suction line is barely cool, you hear hissing from the coil area)
- Ice extends to the outdoor unit refrigerant lines
- The blower wheel is visibly damaged or making grinding/scraping noises

**Schedule service for:**
- Blower motor running slow (capacitor replacement)
- Evaporator coil cleaning (requires chemical cleaning and special equipment)
- Duct system inspection for crushed or undersized ducts
- TXV or metering device diagnostics
- Annual [AC maintenance tune-up](https://ac-repair.today/services/ac-maintenance/)

## Prevention — The $5 Fix

The single most effective way to prevent frozen coils:

**Change your air filter every 30 days during cooling season (May-October).**

In South Florida, running the AC 8+ months per year, a $5 filter changed monthly prevents 90% of freeze-related service calls. Use a MERV 8 filter — MERV 11+ filters are too restrictive for most residential systems (they starve the coil of airflow) unless your system was specifically designed for them.

**Other prevention steps:**
- Clear vegetation around the outdoor unit (2ft minimum clearance)
- Don't block supply registers with furniture, curtains, or rugs
- Keep indoor doors open or have transfer grilles installed for return airflow
- Schedule annual [professional maintenance](https://ac-repair.today/services/ac-maintenance/) — a technician will check superheat/subcooling, inspect for leaks, and clean the coil
- Ask about a [thermostat installation](https://ac-repair.today/services/thermostat-installation/) that alerts you to abnormal temperature splits

## Why South Florida Sees More Frozen Coils Than the Rest of the Country

| Factor | Why It Matters |
|--------|----------------|
| **High humidity** | Evaporator runs colder to dehumidify — narrower margin before freezing |
| **Long cooling season** | Filters clog faster (10+ months vs 4-5 in northern states) |
| **Attic-installed air handlers** | Ducts and equipment in 140°F attics suffer faster material degradation |
| **Salt air (coastal areas)** | Corrodes coil fins, reduces heat transfer efficiency, causing coil to run colder |
| **Oversized systems** | Common in track homes where builder chose the cheapest unit — short cycles don't dehumidify but do freeze |
