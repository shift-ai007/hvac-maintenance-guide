# Choosing and Using a Thermostat in South Florida

Your thermostat is the command center of your AC system. In South Florida, where cooling accounts for 50–60% of your electric bill, the right thermostat — programmed correctly — can save $150–$300 per year.

## Types of Thermostats

### Manual (Non-Programmable)

The basic dial or slider thermostat. You set a temperature and it stays there until you change it.

**Problem in South Florida**: Most people set it to 74°F and forget it. The AC runs at full capacity during the hottest part of the day (2–6 PM) when you may not even be home. You're paying peak rates to cool an empty house.

### Programmable

Allows you to set different temperatures for different times of day and days of the week. A basic 7-day programmable thermostat costs $30–$60.

**Recommended South Florida schedule**:

| Time | Temperature | Reason |
|---|---|---|
| 6 AM – 8 AM | 76°F | Morning routine — house is already cool from overnight |
| 8 AM – 5 PM | 82°F | Away at work — no need for 74°F in an empty house |
| 5 PM – 10 PM | 77°F | Home and active — pre-cool 30 minutes before arrival |
| 10 PM – 6 AM | 78°F | Sleeping — ceiling fan creates equivalent of 3–4°F cooler |

### Smart Thermostats

Wi-Fi connected thermostats that learn your habits, respond to your location, and provide energy reports. Popular options: Google Nest ($180–$250), ecobee ($190–$250), Honeywell T9 ($170–$200).

**Features that matter most in South Florida**:
- **Geofencing** — Automatically switches to "away" mode when everyone's phones leave the house. Prevents the biggest waste: cooling an empty home to 74°F
- **Humidity control** — Runs the AC longer to dehumidify even after reaching the temperature setpoint. Critical in South Florida where humidity causes mold
- **Energy reports** — Monthly breakdown showing exactly when and why you used the most electricity
- **Remote access** — Forgot to set it to "away" before a weekend trip? Adjust from your phone

**Features that don't matter much here**:
- Heating schedules (used maybe 10 days per year)
- Hot water integration (not applicable to most Florida homes)

## Installation Considerations

### C-Wire Compatibility

Smart thermostats need a "C wire" (common wire) for constant power. Most South Florida homes built after 2000 have one. If yours has only 4 wires (R, G, Y, W), you'll need:
- A C-wire adapter kit ($15–$30)
- An add-a-wire device
- Or a thermostat that works without one (some Nest and Honeywell models)

An HVAC technician can add a C-wire during installation for $75–$150.

### Location Matters

A thermostat in the wrong spot gives false readings, causing your AC to run too much or too little.

**Bad locations**:
- Exterior walls (heated by the sun — reads higher than actual room temperature)
- Near supply vents (cold air hitting the sensor — reads lower, under-cools the house)
- Direct sunlight (west-facing wall near a window)
- Above heat sources (TV, lamp, kitchen)
- In a closed hallway (poor air circulation — reads stale air temperature)

**Good locations**:
- Interior wall in a frequently used room
- Central location in the home
- 52–60 inches above the floor
- Away from doors and windows

If your thermostat is in a bad location, relocating it costs $100–$200. The energy savings from accurate readings pay for it within a few months.

## Key Settings for South Florida

### Fan Mode: AUTO, Not ON

Setting the fan to "ON" runs the blower 24/7, which:
- Adds $30–$50/month to your bill
- Pulls humidity BACK into the house from the wet evaporator coil when the compressor isn't running
- Defeats the purpose of dehumidification

Keep it on "AUTO" — the fan runs only when the compressor is actively cooling and dehumidifying.

### Never Turn the AC Completely Off

When leaving for vacation, set it to 82°F — not off. In South Florida humidity, an unoccupied home can reach 85°F and 80%+ relative humidity in hours. This leads to:
- Mold growth on walls, furniture, and in ductwork
- Moisture damage to electronics and wood
- Musty smell that takes days to eliminate

82°F maintains basic dehumidification without wasting energy.

### Humidity Setpoint

If your thermostat has a separate humidity setting, set it to 55% relative humidity. This is important because:
- An oversized AC can reach the temperature setpoint quickly while indoor humidity stays at 65%+
- Without a humidity setpoint, the AC shuts off based on temperature alone, leaving the air feeling clammy
- At 55%, the system runs longer when needed to properly dehumidify

## When to Upgrade

Consider upgrading your thermostat if:
- You have a manual thermostat and forget to adjust it daily
- Your electric bills seem higher than they should be
- You leave home on irregular schedules (non-programmable can't handle this)
- Your home feels clammy even when the AC is running (need humidity control)
- You want to track energy usage over time

A $200 smart thermostat that saves $150/year pays for itself in 16 months.
