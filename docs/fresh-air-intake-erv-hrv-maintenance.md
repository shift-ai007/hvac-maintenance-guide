# Fresh Air Intake & ERV/HRV Maintenance for South Florida Homes

As modern South Florida homes get tighter and more energy-efficient, indoor air quality problems that older drafty houses never had are becoming common. Trapped humidity, lingering cooking and cleaning odors, elevated CO₂ from a sealed envelope — these are the side effects of a well-insulated home. The solution many builders and homeowners are turning to is a mechanical fresh-air ventilation system: an Energy Recovery Ventilator (ERV) or Heat Recovery Ventilator (HRV). These systems bring in filtered outdoor air while recovering the energy you've already paid to cool or heat, and they work brilliantly in our climate — but only if you maintain them.

## ERV vs. HRV — Which One Does South Florida Need?

The difference matters for maintenance because the two types handle moisture differently:

- **ERV (Energy Recovery Ventilator)** — Transfers both heat and *moisture* between outgoing stale air and incoming fresh air. In South Florida's humid climate, an ERV pre-dehumidifies the incoming air by transferring humidity from the fresh stream to the outgoing airstream. This is the right choice for our region. An ERV reduces the latent load on your AC, and it keeps indoor humidity in check even when the AC isn't running hard.

- **HRV (Heat Recovery Ventilator)** — Transfers heat only. It brings in outdoor humidity as-is. In a humid climate like Miami-Dade or Broward, an HRV feeds humid outdoor air directly into your return duct, adding to the load your AC has to remove. HRVs make sense in cold, dry climates but are generally a downgrade for South Florida unless paired with a dedicated dehumidifier.

Most South Florida installations use ERVs, and that's what this guide assumes. If your house has an HRV instead, the maintenance steps are identical — just be aware that your humidity balance will need closer monitoring.

## The Maintenance Schedule That Matters

ERVs and HRVs are mechanical systems with moving parts, filters, and a heat-exchange core that gets dirty. Unlike a duct system that can run for years with no attention, a ventilation system needs regular care or it stops ventilating.

### Every 3 Months — Filter Check

ERVs have two filters: one for incoming outdoor air and one for the exhaust airstream. In South Florida, the outdoor-air filter is a high-maintenance item. Pollen, dust, construction particles, and — depending on your neighborhood — sea salt or wildfire smoke all pass through it.

1. Locate the filter access panel on the ERV cabinet. It is usually near the unit in the attic, garage, or mechanical closet.
2. Remove both filters and inspect them against light. If you cannot see light clearly through the media, they need replacement.
3. Replace with the correct size and MERV rating (typically MERV 8 for most residential ERVs). Never use a higher MERV rating than the manufacturer specifies — the fan cannot overcome the airflow resistance of a MERV 13 filter, and the system will move less fresh air than required by code.
4. Mark the date on the new filter frame with a permanent marker.

### Every 6 Months — Core Inspection

The heat-exchange core is a block of alternating air channels — one direction carries stale indoor air out, the perpendicular channels carry fresh outdoor air in. Over months, dust, grease, and moisture build up on the core surfaces, insulating the heat-exchange material and reducing efficiency.

To inspect the core:
1. Slide the core out of the ERV cabinet. In most units, it comes out as a single block held by a latch or clip.
2. Hold it up to light. If the channels look dusty, clogged, or covered in a sticky film, the core needs cleaning.
3. Vacuum loose dust with a soft brush attachment.
4. For grease or biofilm (common in kitchens or high-humidity attics), rinse the core with lukewarm water only — never soap, never a pressure washer. Soap residue traps more dirt, and high pressure damages the plastic channel walls.
5. Let the core dry completely before reinstalling. Reinstall with the airflow-direction arrow facing the same way it came out.

### Annually — Drain and Condensate Check

ERVs in humid climates produce condensate — sometimes a lot of it. The moisture removed from the incoming airstream drains out through a small port. In a Florida attic that hits 140°F in summer, that drain line is a prime spot for algae growth and clogs. A blocked ERV drain can back water into the cabinet, and from there into your ceiling.

- Pour a cup of distilled water or a vinegar-and-water mix through the drain pan to confirm it flows freely.
- If it trickles or backs up, clear the drain line with a wet/dry vacuum at the exterior outlet or use a condensate-safe cleaning tablet.
- Inspect the drain line insulation. Foam on ERV drains crumbles the same way AC lineset insulation does — wrap any exposed bare tubing to prevent sweating.

### Every 3 Years — Damper and Motorized Actuator Check

If your ERV has motorized dampers for "fresh air" or "recirculate" modes (many newer units do), the damper blade can stick or the actuator can fail. The symptom is simple: you hear the fan running but the air in the house feels stale, or a CO₂ monitor shows elevated levels. A stuck-closed damper means zero fresh air delivery regardless of how well you maintain the rest of the system. A technician can verify damper operation by measuring airflow at the intake hood or by observing the actuator linkage during a call for fresh air.

## Signs Your ERV Needs Professional Attention

- **Frozen core in winter** — If the incoming air is very cold and the core freezes, the system may need a frost-control damper adjustment or the intake location may need to be moved away from the roofline where cold air pools.
- **Persistent humidity spikes** — If your indoor RH jumps above 60% when the ERV runs, the moisture-transfer membrane may be compromised or the unit may need a balance adjustment (more exhaust than supply to maintain a slight negative pressure).
- **Reduced airflow from supply registers** — A filter, core, or duct restriction. If all filters are clean, the fan motor or wheel may need service.
- **Musty odor from fresh-air vents** — The intake hood outside may have debris, bird nests, or a dead animal. The outdoor hood should be inspected and cleaned annually.

## Bottom Line

An ERV is the single best investment a tight South Florida home can make for indoor air quality — but it is not a set-and-forget appliance. Change the filters quarterly, rinse the core every six months, clear the condensate drain annually, and you will keep fresh air moving without dumping humidity into the house or paying for energy you already spent cooling the air you're about to throw away.
