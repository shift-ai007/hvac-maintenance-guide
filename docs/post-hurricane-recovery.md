# Post-Hurricane AC Recovery: A Step-by-Step Guide for South Florida Homeowners

The storm has passed. The sun is back. And the last thing on your mind is your air conditioner — until you walk into a 95-degree house and realize the power is back but your AC won't start.

Hurricane season runs June through November in South Florida, and the weeks after a major storm are when most HVAC emergency calls happen. Not because the storm damaged the unit directly, but because the recovery sequence was wrong, or the grid instability did more harm than the wind.

Here's the post-storm AC recovery playbook our team has seen work best over the years.

## The Golden Rule: Wait Before You Start

When the power comes back after an extended outage, **everyone** turns their AC on at the same time. This creates a massive load spike on a grid that just went through trauma. The result? Brownouts, tripped breakers, and compressor damage from low-voltage operation.

**Wait 30-60 minutes** after grid power is restored before starting your AC. This allows:

- Voltage to stabilize across the local grid
- Refrigerant pressures to equalize in your system
- The compressor oil to settle back where it needs to be

Starting a hot compressor against high head pressure is the #1 cause of post-storm compressor failure. That extra half hour makes the difference between a working AC and a $4,500 replacement.

## The Systematic 4-Step Restart

### Step 1: Visual Check Before Power

Before flipping any switches, do a walk-around:

- **Outdoor unit**: Any visible dents, tilted base, or debris inside the condenser grille? Storm debris can punch through the fins. If a branch went through the coil, you'll see refrigerant oil — a dark, greasy stain around the impact point.
- **Disconnect box**: Check that the electrical disconnect (the gray box on the wall near the condenser) is dry inside. Water intrusion here causes shorts when you flip the switch.
- **Condensate line**: Look at the PVC pipe near your indoor unit. If it's clogged with storm debris, water backs up and trips the float switch — the system will seem dead when it's just a safety cutoff.
- **Flood check**: If the outdoor unit was submerged **at all**, do not turn it on. Floodwater compromises the compressor electrical windings and the capacitor. Call a professional.

### Step 2: Restore Power in Order

1. **Main breaker**: Confirm your home has power (lights, fridge running)
2. **AC breaker**: Reset the double-pole breaker for your AC system
3. **Disconnect switch**: Flip the outdoor disconnect to ON
4. **Wait**: 30 minutes minimum before proceeding

### Step 3: First Startup

Set the thermostat to 78°F — not 72. The system is designed to cool gradually. Asking it to pull a 95°F house down to 72°F immediately keeps the compressor running at maximum discharge pressure for hours, which stresses components and runs up your bill.

Listen for:
- A smooth hum from the compressor (no grinding or clicking every few seconds)
- Airflow from vents within 2-3 minutes
- The outdoor fan spinning freely

Anything else — a banging sound, the breaker tripping immediately, a burning smell — means you need a pro.

### Step 4: Monitor for the First Hour

After the system starts, watch for these patterns specific to post-storm conditions:

- **Short cycling** (turns on and off every 2-3 minutes): Usually low refrigerant from a debris-impact leak, or a dirty condenser coil clogged with wind-blown debris
- **Running but not cooling**: Could be a refrigerant leak, or the condenser fan motor took on water during the storm
- **Ice forming on lines**: Restricted airflow — check the filter (it may be wet if there was roof leakage) and the indoor coil

## Generator Connection: What to Know Before You Plug In

If you're running a generator during extended post-hurricane outages, a few HVAC-specific points:

### Portable Generators

Most portable generators (5,000-7,500 watts) can run a well-maintained 3-4 ton AC system — **if** you manage the startup load. An AC unit draws 3-5x its running wattage during the first few seconds of startup (locked rotor amps). A 4-ton unit might run at 4,000W but spike to 15,000W at startup.

**Tips that work:**
- Start the generator and let it stabilize for 5 minutes before connecting the AC
- Turn off all other heavy loads (water heater, oven, washer) when the AC starts
- If the generator bogs down or the AC trips, turn off the AC breaker, wait 5 minutes, and try again. Some units accept a hard-start kit that reduces the startup spike

### Whole-Home Standby Generators

If you're considering a standby generator for next season, size matters for AC coverage. A 22kW unit handles most 3-4 ton systems with room for essentials (fridge, lights, well pump). A 14kW unit may force you to choose between AC and the rest of the house.

## When Post-Storm Recovery Needs Professional Help

Some post-storm issues don't have a DIY fix:

- **Submerged compressor**: As mentioned, floodwater destroys the internal insulation. The compressor must be replaced — an oil change won't fix it.
- **Refrigerant leak from debris**: A branch or flying debris that punctures the line set or condenser coil means the refrigerant charge is gone. The leak must be repaired, the system evacuated, and a fresh charge installed.
- **Electrical damage from surge**: Even with the system off, lightning strikes can induce voltage in the lines and fry the control board or contactor. If the system gets power but the thermostat shows nothing — or the indoor unit runs but the outdoor doesn't — the control board may need replacement.
- **Flooded indoor unit**: If your air handler is in the attic or basement and took on water, the insulation, blower motor, and electrical connections all need inspection. Running a flooded air handler is a fire risk.

## Preparation for Next Storm

Once your AC is running again, use this as a checklist for next season:

1. **[Schedule a professional AC maintenance tune-up](https://ac-repair.today/services/ac-maintenance/)** before next June — a clean system handles restart stress better
2. **Elevate the outdoor unit** if it's in a flood-prone area (concrete pads work, cinder blocks are a fast upgrade)
3. **Install a surge protector** at the disconnect — not just a power strip. A whole-home or AC-specific surge suppressor costs under $200 installed and prevents board-level damage from grid spikes
4. **Document your system** now, not during a storm: take photos of the data plate, know your breaker location, test your generator connection while the weather is clear
5. **Keep a spare air filter** in your hurricane kit — post-storm air quality degrades from mold spores and debris, and you'll want to change the filter more frequently
