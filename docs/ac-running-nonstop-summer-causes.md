# Why Your AC Runs Non-Stop in Summer: Root Causes and Fixes for South Florida Homes

**Author:** AC Repair Today Technical Team  
**Last Updated:** July 2026

---

## It's 3 PM in July. Your AC hasn't shut off in hours. Is this normal?

If you live in South Florida, you've asked yourself this question at least once. Miami-Dade, Broward, and Palm Beach counties share one thing in common during summer: brutal, unrelenting heat and humidity that pushes every air conditioning system to its absolute limit.

An AC system running continuously when outdoor temps exceed 95°F is not automatically a problem — modern systems are designed for extended run cycles. But there's a line between "it's working hard because it's 98°F outside" and "something is wrong." Let me walk you through the difference, based on what we see on thousands of service calls across South Florida every summer.

## When Continuous Running Is Normal

First, the baseline. A properly sized AC system in South Florida will run **50-80% of the time** during peak summer months. That means in an 8-hour stretch, it might run for 4-6 hours total, cycling on and off every 15-30 minutes depending on the outdoor temperature and your thermostat setting.

On **extreme days** — when it's 95°F+ and humid — continuous running for 1-2 hours is normal while the system catches up after a setback period or tries to recover from the afternoon heat load. After that, you should see at least brief cycles where the system satisfies the thermostat and shuts off.

**When to be concerned:** Your system runs for 3+ hours without stopping, never catches up to the set temperature, or runs 24/7 without any off cycle at all.

## The 7 Most Common Reasons Your AC Won't Stop Running

### 1. Undersized System for South Florida's Heat Load

This is the most common hidden issue we diagnose. When a contractor installs an AC system using "rule of thumb" sizing (square footage only) instead of performing a Manual J load calculation, the system ends up undersized for the actual heat gain. South Florida's solar radiation, humidity, and afternoon sun exposure add enormous load that basic sizing doesn't capture.

**Fix:** A professional load calculation. If the system is genuinely undersized, the options are adding zone supplementation, improving insulation and window treatment, or — in the worst case — replacing with a correctly sized unit. We've done many of these replacements and written in detail about the process at [AC Repair Today](https://ac-repair.today).

### 2. Dirty Air Filter Restricting Airflow

This one is trivial to fix but accounts for roughly 20% of "AC won't stop running" calls we handle. A clogged filter starves the evaporator coil of air, which means the refrigerant can't absorb heat efficiently. The system runs longer trying to cool, the coil can freeze, and you end up paying for a service call that a $5 filter change would have prevented.

**South Florida-specific note:** The humidity here loads your filter faster than in drier climates. During summer, check your filter every 2-3 weeks, not monthly. MERV 8 is the sweet spot — anything higher restricts airflow too much for most residential systems.

### 3. Low Refrigerant (Freon Leak)

Here's the one that fools most homeowners: a system low on refrigerant runs constantly, blows air that feels "cool-ish but not cold," and seems to just keep going without ever satisfying the thermostat. The evaporator coil is too cold (below freezing in the spots that still have refrigerant), which actually reduces dehumidification — so your house feels clammy and cool, never comfortably cold.

**This is not a "top off" situation.** If you're low on refrigerant, you have a leak. Period. The EPA requires it to be located and repaired before recharging. We've seen systems lose half their charge through pinhole leaks in outdoor coils — common in coastal areas where salt air accelerates corrosion. Our [emergency AC repair](https://ac-repair.today/services/emergency-ac-repair/) team handles these daily across Miami-Dade.

### 4. Damaged or Dirty Evaporator Coil

The evaporator coil sits inside your air handler and absorbs heat from the air passing over it. Over time, especially in Florida's humid environment, that coil can get coated with grime, dust, and biological growth. When the coil is dirty, heat transfer drops dramatically, and the system runs far longer than it should.

A damaged coil — corroded fins, refrigerant tube leaks, or physical damage — has similar symptoms but requires replacement rather than cleaning. Coastal corrosion from the salt air near beachfront properties is a recurring issue we see in condos and homes from Sunny Isles to Key Biscayne.

### 5. Thermostat Placement and Calibration Issues

If your thermostat is located in a spot that doesn't reflect the average home temperature — direct sunlight, near a kitchen, in a hallway with poor air return — it will read incorrectly and tell the AC to run longer than needed.

A related issue: "anticipator" calibration on older mechanical thermostats. If the anticipator setting doesn't match your system's current draw, the thermostat can delay the off cycle, causing extended runtime. Modern smart thermostats handle this automatically, which is one reason upgrading often reduces runtime.

### 6. Undersized Return Air Ducts

This is the ductwork version of a dirty filter — the system can't pull enough return air, so it runs longer trying to condition a space with insufficient airflow through the coil. The symptom is identical to a dirty filter, but the fix involves duct modifications rather than a quick swap.

**Quick test:** Open the filter slot and remove the filter temporarily for 30 seconds (don't run without one long-term). If the system seems to breathe better and airflow improves at the vents, you may have a return air restriction, not a filter issue.

### 7. Leaky Ductwork Losing Conditioned Air

Duct leakage is epidemic in South Florida homes, particularly in attics. If your ducts leak 20-30% of your conditioned air into the attic — which can easily hit 130°F in July — your AC runs continuously because half the cooling it produces vanishes before reaching your living spaces.

**The fix:** Duct sealing (aerosol-based or manual mastic). Aeroseal technology has made this far more practical, and many utility companies offer rebates for duct sealing in our service area.

## Less Common but Worth Knowing

### Oversized (Short Cycling "Disguise")

Counter-intuitively, an oversized system can appear to run continuously in some cases. Here's how: an oversized system short cycles during mild weather (running 5 minutes, off 10), but during peak heat, it runs longer to make up for the humidity problem it created. Oversized systems don't dehumidify properly because they cool too fast and shut off before moisture is removed. The lingering humidity makes the thermostat read higher than it should (since humid air feels warmer), so the system runs more during peak hours to compensate.

### Failed Run Capacitor

The run capacitor gives the compressor and fan motor the electrical boost they need during operation. When it's failing but not dead, the compressor runs hotter and less efficiently, drawing more power and struggling to maintain head pressure. The system runs longer to achieve the same cooling, and you'll see higher electric bills as a clue.

### Blocked Condenser Coil (Outdoor Unit)

The outdoor unit — typically on the ground or a roof in South Florida — needs unimpeded airflow across its coil. If it's clogged with dust, grass clippings, or the ever-present love vine that seems to grow everywhere here, heat rejection drops and the compressor runs longer. **Check your outdoor unit clearance**: minimum 24 inches on all sides, and the coil should be hosed down gently every spring.

## How to Measure Your AC's Runtime

If you want hard data before calling for help:

1. **Set a stopwatch** — Note the time your system starts a cycle and when it stops. Do this 3-4 times during peak heat (2-5 PM).
2. **Log the outdoor temperature** — If it's over 98°F, give the system some grace.
3. **Check the supply vent temperature** — With a probe thermometer, it should read 14-20°F cooler than the return air temperature (what's being sucked into the filter). Less than 14°F delta = performance problem.
4. **Monitor the thermostat** — Is the setpoint being reached? If you set 74°F and the system runs 4 hours straight but the room only hits 76°F, you have a capacity issue.

## The Bottom Line

An AC running non-stop in South Florida summer warrants investigation — but a quick DIY filter check and outdoor unit inspection might save you a service call. If those check out and the system still runs 3+ hours without cycling off, you're losing capacity somewhere: refrigerant, airflow, or duct leakage in roughly that order of likelihood.

Systems well-maintained with regular service — including annual checkups by a licensed HVAC contractor — run significantly less than neglected ones. A maintained system at 10 years old often cools better than a neglected system at 5 years. It's that simple.
