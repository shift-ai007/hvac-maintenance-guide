# Coastal Corrosion Prevention for HVAC Systems

A practical guide for South Florida homeowners on protecting their AC equipment from salt-air damage.

## Why Coastal Corrosion Matters

If you live within 5 miles of the Atlantic Ocean or Biscayne Bay, your AC system is under constant attack. Salt particles carried by sea breezes settle on metal surfaces — especially the outdoor condenser unit — and begin an electrochemical reaction that never stops.

Unlike inland corrosion that takes 15-20 years to cause visible damage, coastal corrosion in South Florida can destroy a condenser coil in **4-7 years** and a compressor in **8-10 years**. A $10,000 whole-system replacement driven by corrosion that was preventable.

## What Gets Damaged First

### 1. Condenser Coils (The Most Vulnerable Component)

The aluminum fins and copper tubing of your outdoor condenser are the primary target. Salt deposits:

- **Accelerate galvanic corrosion** where aluminum meets copper
- **Create pinhole leaks** in refrigerant lines — gradual at first, then catastrophic
- **Reduce heat transfer efficiency** by 15-25% as corrosion byproducts insulate the coils
- **Trigger high-pressure lockouts** on hot days when corroded coils can't shed heat

### 2. Electrical Connections

Salt air corrodes electrical terminals faster than any other environmental factor:

- **Contactors fail** — the silver alloy contacts pits and arcs
- **Capacitor terminals corrode** — loose connections cause overheating
- **Control board traces erode** — phantom errors and intermittent lockouts
- **Wire insulation becomes brittle** — exposed copper at terminal blocks

### 3. Sheet Metal and Fasteners

The cabinet, fan guard, and mounting hardware all suffer:

- Screws become impossible to remove without drilling
- Panel edges rust and lose weather sealing
- Fan blades become unbalanced from uneven corrosion
- Drain pan rust-through causes ceiling leaks

## The South Florida Difference

Coastal corrosion in South Florida is worse than in other coastal regions because of three compounding factors:

1. **Heat** — Every 18°F increase doubles the corrosion rate. Our 95°F summer days mean reactions happen 5x faster than in Pacific Northwest coastlines
2. **Humidity** — Corrosion requires moisture. Our 80%+ relative humidity keeps the electrolyte layer active even when it hasn't rained
3. **Salt concentration** — Onshore breezes in Miami-Dade carry measurable salt particulate year-round, not just during storms

## Prevention Strategies

### Strategy 1: Coil Protection (Most Effective)

**Installed a factory-coated coil on replacement**: Pre-coated coils (Heresite, E-coat, or similar) cost 15-20% more but last 3-4x longer in coastal environments. On a new system installation, the upgrade is usually $200-400 — trivial compared to replacing an uncoated coil in year 5.

**Aftermarket spray coatings**: Products like Coil-Cote and SpinFin can be applied to existing coils. The unit must be fully cleaned and dry before application. Expect 2-3 additional years of protection per application.

### Strategy 2: Gutter-Style Wash Regimen

The simplest DIY prevention that actually works requires no special products:

1. **Monthly gentle rinse** during dry season, **weekly** during November-May (when winds come from the east)
2. Use a garden hose with a **fan nozzle** — never a pressure washer (it bends fins)
3. Spray from **inside out** — water should flow through the coils from the fan side outward
4. Rinse at **low pressure** — just enough to carry salt off the fins
5. **Avoid noon** — rapid evaporation concentrates the salt. Early morning or evening

This alone can double coil life. Most salt deposits are water-soluble. Washing is cheap and more effective than any chemical treatment.

### Strategy 3: Physical Placement

If you're installing a new system or have the option to relocate:

- **Avoid ocean-facing walls** — place the condenser on the side of the house that faces away from prevailing summer winds (south and west in most of South Florida)
- **Maintain 3+ feet of clearance** — tight corners trap salt-laden air
- **Elevate 6-12 inches** — use a concrete pad or Uni-strut stand, not a ground-level slab. Splash-back from rain carries salt particles up into the coil
- **Consider a roof mount** — rooftop units get better airflow and less salt exposure (but are harder to service and more expensive to install)

### Strategy 4: Active Protection Systems

- **Corrosion-inhibiting filters** — cut-to-fit mesh screens treated with VCI (vapor corrosion inhibitor) placed behind the intake grille. Replace every 3 months. These help but restrict airflow slightly (acceptable for most systems)
- **Sacrificial anodes** — a zinc or magnesium anode wired to the condenser cabinet. It corrodes instead of the copper. Requires inspection every 6 months. Effectiveness varies with humidity levels
- **Bonded aluminum coils** — an upgrade option that replaces copper/aluminum coils with all-aluminum construction. No galvanic reaction possible. More expensive ($400-800 premium) but eliminates the most common corrosion failure path entirely

## Early Detection Checklist

Run this inspection every **three months** (February, May, August, November):

- [ ] **Coil fins**: Any white or green powdery deposits? (Corrosion byproducts)
- [ ] **Copper tubing**: Green discoloration at bends or joints? (Pinpoint leak risk)
- [ ] **Electrical terminals**: Green/white crust at contactor lugs or capacitor terminals?
- [ ] **Fan guard**: Rust spots forming on the wire guard or mounting bolts?
- [ ] **Cabinet**: Rust along the bottom edge or at screw holes?
- [ ] **Drain pan**: Rust-through or standing water? (Pan corrosion accelerates coil corrosion)
- [ ] **Service valves**: Corrosion on Schrader cores? (Refrigerant loss risk)
- [ ] **Sound**: New rattling noises from the outdoor unit? (Fan imbalance from uneven corrosion)

Any two "yes" answers means corrosion is ahead of normal. Schedule a professional evaluation.

## When Prevention Fails

Despite best efforts, coastal units eventually reach a point where replacement beats continued patching:

- **Pinhole leaks in the evaporator** means the indoor coil is corroding — replacement is the only lasting option
- **Compressor windings short to ground** from salt ingress — replacement is mandatory (and generally worth replacing the whole system)
- **Multiple capacitor failures** (3+ in two years) — the salt has compromised the terminal block assembly. A full electrical retrofit or replacement is needed
- **Control board failure from trace corrosion** — after two board replacements, the pattern is established

A licensed [AC contractor in South Florida](https://ac-repair.today/services/ac-repair/) can assess whether repair makes economic sense or if the corrosion pattern has advanced too far. Our coastal environment is hard on equipment — knowing when to replace is as important as knowing how to protect.
