# Summer Power Outage AC Protection for South Florida Homes

June through November is South Florida's hurricane and severe thunderstorm season, bringing frequent power outages that can damage HVAC systems in ways most homeowners never consider until it's too late. This guide covers what happens to your AC when the lights go out and how to prevent expensive repairs.

## Why Power Outages Damage AC Systems

An air conditioning system is an inductive load — the compressor and fan motors rely on stable, in-phase electrical current to operate correctly. When power is restored after an outage, several destructive events can occur:

### 1. Voltage Spikes on Restore

Utility companies sometimes experience a momentary voltage surge when re-energizing a neighborhood after a blackout. This surge — often 150% or more of normal line voltage — can hit the compressor winding insulation before the system has a chance to stabilize. The result is a weakened or shorted compressor, one of the most expensive components to replace in any split-system AC.

### 2. Short Cycling on Return

Many thermostats are still calling for cooling when power returns. The compressor receives the start signal instantly while internal refrigerant pressures are unequal — liquid refrigerant may slug the compressor valves, causing mechanical damage or immediate lockout.

### 3. Brownout Conditions

During the tail end of a storm, utilities often deliver reduced voltage (brownout conditions) before full restoration. Running a compressor at 85-90% rated voltage increases amperage draw significantly. Sustained brownout operation can overheat compressor windings in under 30 minutes.

## How to Protect Your AC System

### Before Storm Season (Preparation)

**Install a whole-home surge protector.** A Type 1 or Type 2 surge protection device (SPD) installed at the main panel costs $200–$400 installed and protects the AC disconnect, condenser, and air handler from voltage spikes. South Florida's frequent lightning alone justifies this investment.

**Verify your shutoff procedure.** Know where the AC disconnect switch is at the exterior condenser. Most units have a non-fused pull-out disconnect within sight of the outdoor unit. Practice pulling it before an actual emergency.

### During a Power Outage

**Turn the thermostat to OFF** or — better — turn the AC's breaker to OFF as soon as the outage begins. This prevents the system from attempting to restart when power returns unevenly. Leave it off until the power has been stable for at least 5–10 minutes.

**Do NOT open windows** thinking the house will cool naturally. In South Florida's humidity, open windows introduce moist air that takes days to remove once power returns and the AC runs again.

### After Power Is Restored

1. Wait at least 5 minutes after stable power is confirmed before restoring AC power.
2. Turn the breaker on first, then set the thermostat to COOL at 2°F above room temperature.
3. Listen for unusual sounds during the first compressor start cycle — grinding, buzzing, or clicking that persists beyond 3 seconds warrants a professional inspection.
4. Check the outdoor unit fan spins freely and the condenser coil is clear of debris (storm-blown leaves and branches are common after a South Florida storm).

## Common Post-Outage AC Problems

| Symptom | Likely Cause | What To Do |
|---------|-------------|------------|
| AC won't start after power returns | Tripped breaker or blown fuse at disconnect | Reset breaker; check 3-5A fuses in disconnect |
| Indoor fan runs, outdoor unit silent | Capacitor failed during surge | Replace dual-run capacitor ($15–30 part) |
| Compressor hums but won't start | Start capacitor or contactor stuck open | Professional diagnosis needed |
| AC runs but no cool air | Refrigerant loss or compressor not engaging | Call for service — likely surge-related |
| Burning smell on first restart | Dust burning off heating strips (heat pump) | Normal if brief; persistent smell = service |

## When to Call a Professional

Any of these conditions after a power outage requires a licensed HVAC technician:

- Breaker trips repeatedly when AC is switched on
- Compressor hums but won't start (especially after a surge)
- Unusual noises persist more than a few seconds
- AC runs for 30+ minutes without meaningful temperature drop
- Burned smell from the air handler or condenser

Post-storm service calls are among the busiest periods for South Florida AC companies. If your system was running fine before the storm and isn't working after, prompt diagnosis can prevent a full compressor replacement.
