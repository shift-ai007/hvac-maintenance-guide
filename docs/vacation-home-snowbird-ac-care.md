# Seasonal & Vacation Home AC Management Guide

South Florida is home to one of the largest seasonal and snowbird populations in the country. If you own a home here that sits empty for weeks or months at a time — or if you rent your property seasonally — your AC system faces conditions that year-round residents never deal with.

An idle AC in South Florida's humidity is at higher risk of failure than one running daily. This guide covers how to protect your system while you are away and what to do when you return.

## The Core Problem: Humidity + Stillness

South Florida's average outdoor relative humidity runs 70–80% year-round. Inside a closed, unairconditioned home, that number climbs to 85%+ within 48 hours. At those levels:

- **Mold growth** begins on organic surfaces within 24–72 hours
- **Wood warps** — flooring, cabinets, and doors absorb moisture and swell
- **Electronic corrosion** accelerates on thermostat contacts, circuit boards, and wire connections
- **Dust mites** thrive and multiply exponentially
- **Musty odors** develop in fabrics, carpets, and drywall

The goal of vacation-home AC management is **humidity control first, temperature second**.

## Setting Your AC While Away

### The "85°F Rule" Is Wrong

Many seasonal homeowners are told to set their AC to 85°F while away. This is dangerously high for South Florida. At 85°F and 80% humidity, the dew point is 78°F — well above the 55°F dew point needed to prevent mold growth. The compressor will rarely run, and your home becomes a mold incubator.

### Recommended Away Settings

| Duration | Thermostat Setpoint | Fan Setting | Notes |
|----------|-------------------|-------------|-------|
| 1–7 days | 80–82°F | AUTO | Standard energy-saver; fine for short absences |
| 1–4 weeks | 78–80°F | AUTO | Keeps humidity below 60% in most homes |
| 1–3 months | 76–78°F | AUTO | Maintains safe humidity; cost is ~$40–60/month extra |
| 3+ months | 74–76°F | AUTO | Full humidity control; protects structure and contents |

The key metric is **indoor relative humidity**, not temperature. Below 60% RH, mold cannot grow. Above 60% RH, you are gambling with your home's interior.

### Use a Thermostat with Remote Monitoring

If your vacation home has a smart thermostat (and it should), set it to send alerts for:
- Temperature above 85°F → AC may have failed
- Humidity above 65% → System is not dehumidifying adequately
- System offline → Power outage or Wi-Fi disconnection

South Florida thunderstorms cause frequent power flickers that can leave a thermostat offline without the homeowner's knowledge. Some smart thermostats offer cellular backup, which is worth the $5–10/month for seasonal properties.

## Pre-Departure Checklist

Before locking up for the season:

### 1. Change the Air Filter

Install a fresh MERV-8 filter the day you leave. A dirty filter restricts airflow, which causes the evaporator coil to freeze and can lead to compressor damage. Do not install a higher-MERV filter — the airflow restriction is worse when the filter is going to sit for months.

### 2. Clear the Condensate Drain

Pour one cup of white vinegar through the condensate drain port (typically a T-valve near the air handler or a port at the drain line exit). This kills any biofilm or algae that has started growing in the line. A clogged drain is the number-one cause of water damage claims in seasonal homes.

### 3. Check the Float Switch

Ensure your secondary drain pan or emergency float switch is working. Pour a cup of water into the secondary drain pan — the float should rise and shut the system off. If the switch is stuck (common after long idle periods), clean the float mechanism with distilled vinegar and a soft brush.

### 4. Inspect Outdoor Unit Clearance

South Florida vegetation grows fast. Trim any plants, shrubs, or weeds within 3 feet of the condenser. A hedge that provides proper clearance in January will be pressing against the cabinet by May.

### 5. Secure the Outdoor Disconnect

If the property will be vacant for months, consider turning the AC off at the outdoor disconnect switch (not the breaker — GFCI and AFCI breakers can trip from storms and leave the indoor unit running with no outdoor compressor). However, only do this **if the indoor thermostat is set to OFF** — never leave the system set to COOL with the outdoor disconnect off.

### 6. Seal the Thermostat

If you are not using remote monitoring, tape a note to the thermostat or the front door that says **"Do not adjust thermostat below 76°F."** Guests, property managers, and well-meaning neighbors often crank the AC to 68°F on arrival, shocking a system that has been running lightly for weeks and risking a frozen coil.

## Returning After an Absence

### Don't Crank It Down Immediately

When you walk into a warm, humid home, the instinct is to set the thermostat to 70°F and wait. **Don't.** Rapid cooling of a humid space causes condensation inside walls, on ducts, and in insulation — creating a mold problem you will not see for months.

Instead:
1. Open windows for 15–30 minutes to equalize indoor and outdoor conditions
2. Close windows and set thermostat to 80°F
3. After 2 hours, lower to 78°F
4. After another 2 hours, lower to your normal setpoint (74–76°F)

This gradual pull-down gives the system time to remove moisture before cooling further.

### Check for Problems Before Full Use

Before the system starts working hard, look for:
- **Musty smell at supply vents** → possible mold in ducts or on the coil
- **No cold air** → refrigerant may have leaked during idle period
- **Loud noise from outdoor unit** → debris or animals may have nested in the condenser
- **Water stains near air handler** → the condensate drain may be clogged
- **Tripped breaker** → power surge during your absence
- **Ice on refrigerant lines** → low charge or restricted airflow

> Any of these signs means you should call for professional [AC repair](https://ac-repair.today/services/ac-repair/) before the system fails entirely. Emergency call-outs are more expensive than a scheduled return-season inspection.

### The First-Run Checklist

After a prolonged absence, run through this sequence:

| Step | Action |
|------|--------|
| 1 | Turn system ON at the thermostat — set to COOL, 78°F |
| 2 | Listen for the compressor and fan to start within 30 seconds |
| 3 | Feel the largest supply vent — air should be noticeably cool within 5 minutes |
| 4 | Check outdoor unit — warm air should blow from the top |
| 5 | Check condensate drain — should drip within 15 minutes of compressor start |
| 6 | After 30 minutes, measure temperature split: 16–22°F difference between return and supply |
| 7 | Install a fresh air filter |
| 8 | Test each zone (if zoned system) for proper cooling |

## Rental Property Considerations

If your South Florida home is a seasonal rental (snowbird owner, summer tenant, or short-term rental):

### Between Tenants
- Change the filter between every tenant — even if the previous rental was only a week. Multiple guests in a short-term rental load filters faster than a family in a permanent home.
- Walk the HVAC schedule at check-in and check-out. Set a reasonable setback (78°F) as the default, not a wide temperature range. Guests who see "65–90°F" will test the lower end.
- Have a contract with a local [AC maintenance](https://ac-repair.today/services/ac-maintenance/) provider for between-tenant inspections during peak season. A small problem caught between guests is a service call; one caught during a guest's stay is an emergency with a refund request.

### Hurricane Season Overlap
If your seasonal absence coincides with hurricane season (June–November), your AC faces double risk. Before leaving:
- Ensure your surge protector is functional — lightning strikes to neighborhood power lines are the leading cause of post-storm AC failure
- Confirm insurance covers AC damage from power surges (most basic policies do not)
- Know the post-hurricane restart procedure: do not run the system if there is visible flood damage to the outdoor unit or if the condensate drain may have backed up

## If the System Fails While You Are Away

A failed AC in a vacant South Florida home creates a cascade of damage:
- **48 hours**: Humidity in the home rises above 70%
- **72 hours**: Mold spores begin germinating on moist surfaces
- **1 week**: Visible mold growth on walls, ceilings, and ductwork
- **2 weeks**: Structural damage to wood framing, drywall, and flooring

If your smart thermostat alerts you to a high-temperature event while you are away:
1. Contact your property manager or a neighbor to check for obvious issues (tripped breaker, outdoor disconnect off, thermostat blank)
2. Authorize a service visit immediately — the cost of an after-hours [emergency AC repair](https://ac-repair.today/services/emergency-ac-repair/) call is far less than the cost of full mold remediation
3. If the system cannot be repaired remotely, have the technician secure the system and set up a dehumidifier on a drain to keep humidity below 55% until you return
